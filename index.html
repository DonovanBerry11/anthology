    Dollar Friendly Fire

  • May 2026

    The Ratchet

    Each year, roughly a third of US Treasury debt matures and must be refinanced. The bonds issued at the historic lows of 2020 are now rolling over at rates three to four times higher. The mechanism is arithmetic, not political — and unlike a deficit or a spending increase, it cannot be reversed by any future administration's decisions.

  • May 2026

    The Subtraction Problem

    US net migration turned negative in 2025 for the first time in decades. This is being covered as an immigration enforcement story. It is also a structural labour economics story, and the arithmetic has no substitute.

    The Regulatory Sovereign

    Europe has constructed the world's most sophisticated regulatory architecture — the AI Act, the Digital Markets Act, the Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism — and remains structurally dependent on American compute, American security guarantees, and American capital markets. At the moment when it faces its greatest strategic demands in a generation — rearmament, energy independence, industrial renewal — the EU's fiscal framework makes the capital required structurally impossible to raise. The regulatory sovereign can govern the markets of others; it cannot, by governing alone, become sovereign over the forces that will determine which societies thrive.

    The Bluff That Held, The Logic That Didn't

    Operation Sindoor has been celebrated as proof that nuclear deterrence between India and Pakistan is robust — India struck deep into Pakistani territory, the threshold held, and the crisis was managed. The argument here is that this triumphalist reading is precisely wrong: both states have now learned through practice that limited war under nuclear shadow is manageable, and that belief, rather than the arsenals themselves, is the primary new source of instability in South Asia.

  • May 2026

    The Worst of Both Worlds

    Trump's economic interventionism is not a version of Chinese state capitalism or European industrial policy — it is political capitalism: a patronage system in which state power functions as an instrument of personal deal-making. The result combines the inefficiency risks of state intervention with the concentration risks of unaccountable private power, without the compensating benefits of either. What it produces is not industrial capacity but oligarchic political economy, structured around relationship rather than rule and considerably harder to dismantle than any policy platform.
