The Summit That Settled Nothing: What Beijing's Silence on Boeing — and Washington's Silence on Chips — Really Signals

The most consequential thing about the Trump-Xi summit in Beijing was what neither side said. President Trump completed a two-day state visit — the first by an American president since 2017 — flanked by a roster of Silicon Valley's heaviest hitters: Tim Cook, Elon Musk, and Jensen Huang all made the trip, a visual designed to project commercial seriousness. What the visit did not produce was any of the substantive movement markets had priced in. Chip stocks slid sharply on Friday as it became clear that no breakthrough on Nvidia's ability to sell advanced semiconductors into China had materialized, and the S&P's tech-heavy components gave back a meaningful portion of the week's gains. The summit's failure to resolve the export control standoff is not a minor footnote — it is the central story, because the entire logic of bringing Jensen Huang to Beijing was to signal that a deal on AI hardware access was at least negotiable. Beijing's silence suggested it wasn't, or not yet. On Taiwan and Iran, the communiqué language was carefully drained of anything actionable, which CSIS analysts noted was itself a choice: studied ambiguity lets both sides claim they didn't concede, while deferring every hard question to future working groups that may never convene. The one concrete deliverable — a tentative Chinese commitment to purchase Boeing aircraft — was received with conspicuous quiet from Beijing's official channels, which typically amplify commercial wins when they want to. That silence matters. It suggests the Boeing line was offered as a face-saving gesture for the American side rather than a genuine near-term procurement signal, and anyone watching Chinese aviation procurement patterns knows that tentative commitments at the head-of-state level have a long and uneven record of translation into actual orders. The Wire China flagged that domestic Chinese sentiment around the summit was notably muted, with state media giving it far less promotional coverage than the 2017 Mar-a-Lago meeting received, a sign that Xi's political incentives around the relationship are more constrained than they were a decade ago. For markets, the read-through is straightforward and uncomfortable: the bilateral relationship remains structurally adversarial on the technology dimension regardless of the atmospherics. The question now is whether the working-group framework the two sides agreed to produces any semiconductor-adjacent movement before the next earnings cycle forces Nvidia to quantify the revenue impact of continued export restrictions.

Fifty-Forty-Nine: The Vote That Measured How Much the Iran War Is Costing Trump at Home

A single vote has become the most precise measurement yet of how much the Iran war is eroding Donald Trump's political coalition at home. The Senate's War Powers Resolution — which would have required congressional authorization before further strikes on Iran — failed 50-49, but the margin is almost beside the point. Three Republicans voted yes, including Lisa Murkowski, who had never previously broken with the administration on the conflict. That is not a rebellion, but it is a signal with a direction, and in a 53-47 chamber the direction matters more than the current count. Each of the seven War Powers votes since the conflict began has drawn slightly more Republican support than the last, a trend line that NPR noted is attracting attention in both party whip operations. The political pressure behind these votes is not primarily ideological — it is constituent-driven. A Reuters/Ipsos poll published alongside the vote found that two-thirds of American voters say Trump has not clearly explained the rationale for the conflict, a number that is striking not because it reflects Democratic opposition but because it almost certainly captures a substantial share of independent and soft-Republican voters whose support the party needs in the 2026 midterm environment. The economics are doing as much work as the polling. The Strait of Hormuz remains effectively closed, with Iran maintaining its blockade even as a nominal ceasefire holds — a ceasefire that has not, in practice, reopened the chokepoint through which roughly 20% of the world's traded oil flows. Al Jazeera's reporting from the region paints a picture of a waterway in legal limbo: the guns are mostly quiet but the tankers are not moving, and every additional week of closure compounds the inflationary pressure that is already forcing the incoming Fed chair into one of the most difficult monetary policy environments in four decades. The domestic political math is what makes the 50-49 vote worth watching closely. Trump retains the ability to veto any War Powers resolution that clears Congress, and a veto-proof majority remains a distant prospect. But the threshold for Republican defection appears to be falling, and if the Hormuz closure produces another leg up in gasoline prices before the summer driving season peaks, the political cost of the war — measured in congressional votes rather than battlefield outcomes — will almost certainly rise further.

The Warsh Fed Inherits a Stagflation Trap: What the Powell-to-Warsh Handoff Means for Rates, Markets, and the Oil Shock

Jerome Powell leaves the Federal Reserve having navigated a pandemic, the fastest tightening cycle in four decades, and a soft landing that most economists thought was structurally impossible — and he is handing his successor a situation that may be harder than any of them. Kevin Warsh inherits the chairmanship at a moment when the Fed's dual mandate has become a genuine trap rather than a theoretical tension. March CPI came in at 3.3% year-over-year, up sharply from 2.4% in February, with gasoline prices rising 21.2% in a single month as the Iran conflict and the effective closure of the Strait of Hormuz work their way through the energy complex. At the same time, the labor market is softening — not collapsing, but trending in a direction that removes the Fed's traditional license to respond to an inflation impulse with aggressive rate increases. The last time the central bank faced this specific combination — supply-driven inflation against a weakening growth backdrop — it was the 1970s, and the institutional memory of that era is now surfacing explicitly in market commentary. Crestwood Advisors noted in a recent client note that the CAPE ratio for the S&P 500 has reached 39.6, a level exceeded in only roughly 3% of months since 1957, meaning equities are priced for a benign outcome at exactly the moment the macro environment is producing anything but. Warsh's own policy instincts are well documented: he dissented in favor of earlier tightening during the post-2008 recovery, which places him ideologically closer to the inflation-fighting tradition than to the growth-accommodating wing of Fed thinking. That might be the right disposition for this moment, or it might be precisely wrong if the energy shock proves transitory and a premature rate increase tips a softening labor market into something worse. The Fed's credibility problem is that it cannot easily communicate which scenario it believes is more likely without either validating inflation fears or unsettling a labor market that is already showing stress. What Warsh's early communication choices signal about his framework — whether he treats the gasoline surge as a supply shock to be looked through or as a second-round inflation risk to be suppressed — will be the most consequential piece of Fed signaling since the 2022 pivot, and markets will price it in long before any formal policy action follows.

The Other Chip Story: What Cerebras's 68% Debut Reveals About the AI Infrastructure Race Beyond Nvidia

Cerebras Systems did in a single trading session what most IPO candidates spend months failing to do: it gave investors a reason to believe the AI infrastructure story has a second chapter. The company's Nasdaq debut on May 14 produced a 68% first-day gain, pushing its market capitalization to approximately $95 billion before a modest pullback in Friday's broader tech selloff — itself triggered, in part, by the absence of any Nvidia export breakthrough at the Beijing summit. The timing is not incidental. Cerebras builds AI chips architecturally distinct from Nvidia's GPU paradigm — its wafer-scale design concentrates enormous compute on a single chip rather than linking arrays of smaller ones — and the investment thesis rests precisely on the idea that the AI buildout is large and urgent enough to support multiple infrastructure bets simultaneously. That thesis found an audience. CNBC noted that the order book for the offering was heavily oversubscribed, reflecting institutional conviction that the current moment of Nvidia export uncertainty actually expands the addressable market for credible alternatives rather than simply disadvantaging the entire sector. There is a logic to that reading. If the U.S.-China technology standoff continues to restrict Nvidia's ability to sell its most capable chips into one of the world's largest AI markets, Chinese cloud providers and research institutions will accelerate their own domestic chip programs, and American competitors without Nvidia's export exposure become strategically interesting in a way they previously were not. Cerebras has consistently positioned itself as a high-performance alternative for inference workloads specifically — the compute-intensive process of running trained AI models — which is increasingly where the commercial volume sits as foundation models mature. TheStreet flagged that the company's revenue trajectory, while impressive by growth-stage standards, remains small relative to the valuation the market assigned on debut, which means the IPO price reflects a bet on market capture rather than current fundamentals. That gap between price and fundamentals is not unusual for platform-layer technology at an early stage, but it does mean the stock will be sensitive to any signal — positive or negative — about the pace of enterprise AI infrastructure deployment in the next two to three quarters. Whether Friday's pullback was noise or the beginning of a valuation reset is the question worth tracking as the post-lockup period approaches.

Home Field, Enormous Stakes: What the 30-Day World Cup Countdown Means for American Soccer — and American Business

American soccer has spent thirty years building toward a moment like this, and it arrives in thirty days. The 2026 FIFA World Cup — hosted across the United States, Canada, and Mexico in a format expanded to 48 teams for the first time in the tournament's history — is now close enough that the logistical realities are snapping into focus faster than the sporting ones. FIFA's confirmation of a halftime show at MetLife Stadium for the final is a genuine landmark: nothing like it has existed in World Cup history, and its presence signals how deliberately the tournament's commercial architecture has been designed for an American audience, blending the visual grammar of the Super Bowl with the global scale of the sport itself. The headliners have not been formally announced as of this dispatch, but the framing around the confirmation — global acts, plural — suggests an ambition calibrated to the broadcast reach of a final that will be watched by an audience measured in the hundreds of millions.

The commercial stakes for American soccer specifically are harder to overstate. Goal.com has tracked the sponsorship and broadcast valuations attached to this cycle, and the numbers represent a structural step-change from any previous U.S.-hosted soccer property. For Major League Soccer, a strong performance by the U.S. men's national team in the group stage and beyond would arrive at exactly the moment the league is attempting to convert casual domestic interest into durable fandom — the kind that survives the tournament's end and shows up in season ticket sales and kit revenue. The pressure on the USMNT squad is therefore not purely athletic. Home-tournament performances have a documented effect on youth participation and adult viewership in host nations, and American soccer's development pipeline is still young enough that the next decade of growth could be meaningfully shaped by what happens between June and July. The roster decisions being made now — which MLS players earn minutes, how Christian Pulisic and the European contingent are integrated into the system — will determine whether the team is built for the tournament they are entering or the legacy they are trying to create. The final squad announcement and the first group-stage draw reactions will be the clearest early indicators of whether the national team's ambitions match the moment the sport has been handed.
