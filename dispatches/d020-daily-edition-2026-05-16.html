A Summit Without a Settlement: What the Beijing Talks Did — and Didn't — Resolve

Donald Trump left Beijing with a handshake and a photo opportunity, but none of the agreements the world was watching for. After two days of summit meetings with Xi Jinping, the two leaders issued statements emphasising stability and mutual economic interest, yet departed without resolving any of the three disputes that have defined the tension between their countries: the status of Taiwan, Iran's nuclear posture, and the ongoing closure of the Strait of Hormuz. The Associated Press reported that Trump told reporters he had not yet decided whether to proceed with a major arms package for Taiwan, saying he wanted to give Xi's concerns further consideration — a remark that was received in Taipei with considerable unease and in Beijing with something closer to satisfaction. CSIS analysts noted that the summit's lack of concrete deliverables is itself a kind of outcome, one that leaves the structural conflicts between Washington and Beijing intact while giving both governments the domestic optics of engagement. The Strait of Hormuz has remained effectively closed to commercial oil traffic since February, a fact that has kept global crude prices above one hundred dollars per barrel for months and generated sustained pressure on allied economies from Tokyo to Berlin. Whether the two leaders held more substantive private discussions than what was reported publicly remains unclear, though several senior administration officials briefed afterward were described as guardedly optimistic without offering specifics. What is clear is that the summit did not produce the kind of written framework that would bind either side to a course of action — which, for a conflict as layered as the Iran situation, means the most consequential decisions remain entirely in the hands of individual leaders rather than durable institutions. The question that now presses forward is whether this kind of high-level contact, absent binding outcomes, is enough to prevent miscalculation as the situation in the Gulf continues to evolve.

The Constitution's Blind Spot: Congress Cannot Find the Votes to Reclaim Its War Authority

Congress has now failed three times to formally assert its authority over a war that is already months old. The House voted 212 to 212 on a war powers resolution that would have required President Trump to halt military operations against Iran, with the tie effectively killing the measure — a result that Democracy Now and NBC News both reported came after a single Democrat crossed the aisle to join the Republican bloc in opposition. Under the Constitution and the 1973 War Powers Act, Congress holds the authority to declare war and, theoretically, to terminate unauthorised hostilities after sixty days. In practice, that mechanism has rarely constrained a sitting president, and the current impasse suggests it may now be functionally inoperative. The more alarming development in the week's reporting was the disclosure, sourced to Pentagon discussions, that administration lawyers are evaluating whether renaming the military operation would restart the sixty-day clock under the War Powers Act — a procedural manoeuvre that, if adopted, would represent one of the more brazen constitutional workarounds in recent memory. Legal scholars contacted by NBC News described the gambit as legally dubious but politically viable so long as Congress remains deadlocked. The deeper problem the vote exposed is structural rather than tactical: the narrow partisan margins in the House mean that any member willing to break with their caucus holds effective veto power over the chamber's ability to act as an institution. For the war powers framework to mean anything, it requires a Congress willing to enforce it, and by that measure, the framework is currently not functioning. The coming weeks will test whether the administration moves forward with the renaming strategy and whether that provocation is enough to shift the political calculus for even a handful of members who have so far declined to cross party lines.

Record Highs, Real Pain: How Markets and Main Street Are Living in Different Economies

The stock market and the grocery store are describing two entirely different countries right now. On May 14th, U.S. equity markets closed at record levels, with the S&P 500 surpassing 7,500 for the first time and the Dow recapturing the 50,000 mark, moves driven by optimism surrounding the Trump-Xi summit and a strong earnings season for artificial intelligence companies, according to reporting from TheStreet and Bloomberg. By the conventional measures that Wall Street tracks, the economy looks robust: corporate profits are up, volatility is contained, and major institutional investors are rotating into growth positions. The picture at the consumer level is considerably grimmer. The Bureau of Labor Statistics reported that the consumer price index rose 3.8 percent year-on-year in April, the highest reading since 2023, with fuel and food costs, both significantly elevated by the ongoing conflict in the Gulf, accounting for the largest share of the increase. Deloitte Insights analysts pointed out that this divergence between asset prices and consumer purchasing power is not simply a statistical curiosity; it reflects a genuine bifurcation in economic experience between households that hold significant financial assets and those whose budgets are primarily exposed to goods and services prices. The Federal Reserve, caught between an economy producing record equity valuations and one generating real-wage erosion, has effectively been frozen. Futures markets are now pricing a 74.5 percent probability that the Fed holds rates unchanged through the remainder of 2026, according to Bloomberg data, a posture that would keep mortgage rates elevated and continue squeezing the housing market at the lower end. Whether the ceasefire optimism that moved markets this week translates into actual relief at the fuel pump will determine whether this divergence begins to close or widens further into an election year.

The Agentic Reckoning: When AI Stops Being a Tool and Starts Being the Workforce

The restructuring that technology executives have discussed in theoretical terms for years is now happening at a scale that is impossible to dismiss as routine cost-cutting. In May 2026, Cloudflare announced it was eliminating more than 1,100 positions — approximately 20 percent of its entire workforce — after company data showed internal AI usage had grown 600 percent in the span of three months, effectively automating a significant portion of what those roles had been doing. Amazon separately announced roughly 16,000 corporate job cuts tied explicitly to a strategic shift toward what the company is calling agentic AI workflows, in which autonomous systems handle multi-step processes that previously required human coordination and judgment. CNBC and Yahoo Finance both reported that the pattern repeating across these companies is structurally consistent: AI is no longer being deployed as an enhancement to existing teams but as a direct replacement for entire layers of knowledge work. The BeInCrypto analysis of the broader wave noted that roles in customer support, content operations, software quality assurance, and mid-level data processing are disappearing at a pace that is outrunning the policy and retraining infrastructure designed to absorb displaced workers. What makes this moment different from previous automation cycles is the speed of substitution and the credential level of the workers affected. Earlier waves of automation primarily displaced manual and clerical labor over decades; the current transition is eliminating roles that required university degrees and years of specialised experience within the span of a corporate planning cycle. Economists disagree sharply about whether new job categories will emerge fast enough to reabsorb this cohort, and the honest answer is that no one has reliable data yet — which means the social and political consequences of this reckoning are still accumulating faster than the frameworks for understanding them.

V.

A Warmer Floor: Why This El Niño Is Different From Every One Before It

Every El Niño that arrives now arrives on top of a planet that is already warmer than any El Niño has encountered before. Climate scientists issued warnings this week that the Pacific oscillation developing in the second half of 2026 could be powerful enough, layered on an already elevated global temperature baseline, to drive weather extremes beyond anything in the instrumental record and potentially push 2026 to the warmest year since measurements began, according to reporting from Climate Change News and Earth.Org. The mechanism is well understood: El Niño events add a pulse of heat to global average temperatures, and when that pulse is applied to a baseline that has itself shifted upward through decades of greenhouse gas accumulation, the resulting peaks reach levels that previous El Niño cycles, on a cooler planet, never approached. The human cost of that shift is not hypothetical. A World Weather Attribution study released this week found that the devastating late-April heatwave across India and Pakistan — which brought temperatures above 46 degrees Celsius to densely populated regions and killed at least 47 people — is now three times more likely to occur than it was in the pre-industrial era, and that events of equivalent severity can now be expected to recur roughly every five years. That frequency is not compatible with the adaptation pace of the infrastructure, agriculture, or public health systems in the affected countries. The scientists involved in the attribution study were careful to note that probability statements of this kind describe a changed risk environment, not a prediction of any specific event — but the direction of change is unambiguous, and the arrival of a significant El Niño on top of that changed environment means the coming months of extreme weather will be worth watching with close and serious attention.
