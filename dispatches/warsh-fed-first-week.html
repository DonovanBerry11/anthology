Warsh's First Week: No Rate Cuts This Year, Possibly Ever Under Trump's Terms

May 2026 · Published 18 May 2026, 1:00 PM EST

Kevin Warsh became the 17th chair of the Federal Reserve on May 15, confirmed by the Senate 54-45 in the closest Fed confirmation vote of the modern era, inheriting an institution that his own nominating president has put under sustained pressure to cut rates — and an inflation environment that makes those cuts structurally difficult to deliver.

The data Warsh walked into on day one: CPI at 3.8% — the highest reading since May 2023 — driven partly by oil prices near $115 per barrel as the Strait of Hormuz crisis persists, and partly by the tariff architecture erected by the Trump administration itself. The structural irony is immediate: the president who demanded rate cuts has, through trade policy and the geopolitical consequences of the Iran campaign, created the inflation that prevents them.

At his April 21 Senate Banking Committee confirmation hearing, Warsh was explicit about his priorities. "Inflation is a choice, and the Fed must take responsibility for it," he said, while emphasising that Fed independence was non-negotiable. He told senators he had made the president no promises on rates. The statement set up the structural conflict now visible in markets: Trump nominated Warsh expecting a rate-cut ally; Warsh signalled he would run the institution on its own terms.

Markets have priced this gap. Traders broadly expect rates on hold through all of 2026, with odds of a rate hike rising to around 30% by December — an outcome that would be the political inverse of what Trump anticipated when he made the nomination. Warsh's first FOMC meeting as chair is scheduled for June 16–17; strategists widely expect a hold, with the committee watching whether the tariff-related price pressures prove transitory or begin embedding in services inflation.

The internal dynamics complicate the picture further. Warsh faces what analysts are describing as a "family fight" within the FOMC between governors who lean toward holding and those who would consider easing if labour market conditions deteriorate. His ability to build consensus on the committee — and to maintain a public distance from the White House's rate-cut demands — will define the first months of his tenure.

Watch the June 16–17 FOMC meeting for the first policy statement under Warsh's name, and the accompanying press conference for how he calibrates language on the inflation path and Fed independence.
