April CPI Reaccelerates to 3.8% as Energy Shock Hits Headline

May 2026 · Published 13 May 2026, 12:12 AM EST

The Bureau of Labor Statistics' April CPI release on May 12 showed headline inflation at 3.8% year-over-year, the highest annual print since May 2023, and 0.6% month-over-month against a consensus 3.7%. The energy index rose 3.8% on the month and 17.9% on the year, accounting for roughly forty percent of the all-items increase; gasoline alone was up 28.4% year-over-year, with the national average at the pump above $4.50.

Underneath the energy shock, core CPI — which strips out food and energy — rose 0.4% in April and 2.8% on the year, a tenth above consensus and 0.2 percentage points above March, undermining the read that the inflation is purely supply-driven. CNBC and CNN both note that real average hourly wages slipped 0.5% on the month and 0.3% on the year, meaning that for the first time in three years nominal wage growth is no longer outrunning consumer prices.

The print lands with the Federal Reserve already in an extended hold. The FOMC's April 28–29 decision kept the federal funds rate at 3.5–3.75% with four dissents — the most since 1992 — split between Governor Stephen Miran calling for a quarter-point cut and Hammack, Kashkari, and Logan resisting the inclusion of an easing bias. The median Summary of Economic Projections released in March penciled in one cut for 2026; fed funds futures on CME's FedWatch tool now imply none. The Yahoo Finance composite of post-CPI commentary notes that some Fed watchers are explicitly raising the probability of a rate hike rather than a cut.

The political pressure runs in the opposite direction. Trump has spent the spring publicly demanding lower rates, framing the energy-driven inflation as a supply problem the central bank should "look through." The April data make that posture costlier: core inflation has reaccelerated, and the headline is now far enough above target that the credibility cost of cutting into a 3.8% print would be substantial.

The next data points are the April PCE release on May 30 and the FOMC's June 16–17 meeting; the Cleveland Fed's Inflation Nowcasting model is currently tracking May CPI a tenth above April's headline.
