The Beijing Handshake: What the Trump–Xi Summit Did and Didn't Settle

Donald Trump flew home from Beijing with handshakes, good optics, and very little else. The two-day state visit produced no concrete agreements on Taiwan, no progress on Iran, and no new framework for trade — and yet both governments were eager to call it a success anyway. China's foreign ministry described the meeting as "historical," and Xi Jinping accepted an invitation to visit the United States this fall, which the White House will likely frame as a diplomatic trophy heading into the midterm cycle. Beneath the pleasantries, however, analysts noted something more telling in what Trump did not say: he declined to reaffirm U.S. commitments to Taiwan arms sales, a silence Beijing will almost certainly read as a quiet concession. Reporting from CNN and The Washington Post described the omission as significant, with officials in Taipei watching closely for any signal that Washington's posture is softening. The summit's choreography followed a familiar pattern — elaborate ceremony, carefully worded joint statements, and no binding outcomes — but that does not mean nothing happened. Relationships between superpowers are built as much in register and tone as in treaty language, and both sides appeared willing to arrest a period of accelerating tension. Trade remains the most structurally consequential fault line. Tariffs imposed during earlier rounds of the dispute have not been rolled back, and neither government offered a timeline or mechanism for resolving the underlying disagreements on technology transfer, export controls, and market access. The Taiwan question, meanwhile, is not dormant just because it went unaddressed in Beijing — it has simply been deferred. What the summit did achieve, at minimum, was a restoration of direct leader-to-leader communication at a moment when military-to-military channels remain strained and the risk of miscalculation in the Taiwan Strait is real. For markets, the absence of escalation is itself a form of signal, and the prospect of Xi's fall visit gives both sides a near-term incentive to avoid provocations. Whether Trump's restraint on Taiwan reflects a genuine strategic recalibration or simply the transactional instincts of a president who prefers warm summitry to hard commitments is a question that will likely define the next phase of U.S.-China relations — and Xi's trip to Washington this autumn will be the first real test of the answer.

II.

Countdown to Launch: What SpaceX's Accelerated IPO Means for Markets

SpaceX is not waiting any longer. The company has moved up its long-anticipated IPO to target a June 12 listing on the Nasdaq, according to sources cited by CNBC and reported across Techmeme, compressing a timeline that Wall Street has been speculating about for years into something suddenly and startlingly real. The comparisons to Facebook's 2012 debut are already circulating in analyst notes and trading desks, and they are not entirely hyperbolic — SpaceX is one of the few private companies that has managed to sustain genuine mass fascination while also operating infrastructure that governments depend on, from satellite internet to NASA crew transport. The timing is not accidental. The blockbuster listing of AI chipmaker Cerebras earlier this year reheated appetite for mega-cap tech debuts, and SpaceX's leadership appears to have made a deliberate calculation that the window is open and worth taking. Elon Musk's political profile has complicated the company's public image in some quarters, but SpaceX's revenue base — anchored in Starlink subscriptions, government launch contracts, and Starship development milestones — is arguably more durable than the personal brand questions that trail its founder. Not everyone is optimistic about what a SpaceX listing means for the broader market. Some analysts have warned that an offering of this scale could be actively disruptive, crowding out liquidity from smaller companies and redirecting institutional attention in ways that leave mid-cap and growth names struggling for oxygen in the weeks following the debut. The concern is less about SpaceX itself than about what happens to everything else when a gravitational mass of that size enters a public market. For retail investors, the IPO will arrive with the usual asymmetries — institutional allocations, first-day pricing dynamics, and the long history of high-profile tech debuts that soared, crashed, or simply underwhelmed over longer holding periods. For the space and defense sectors, it will be a genuine valuation event, pricing Starlink's subscriber growth, Starship's commercial potential, and the strategic value of private launch capability into a number that the market will have to live with. June 12 is still weeks away, and IPO timelines have a way of slipping, but if SpaceX makes its Nasdaq debut on schedule, it will be one of the defining market moments of 2026.

The Fed's Impossible Position: Oil Shock, Sticky Inflation, and a Rate Path Going Nowhere

The Federal Reserve entered 2026 hoping inflation was behind it. It was not. With the Strait of Hormuz effectively closed since March following escalating tensions in the Persian Gulf, global oil supply chains have fractured in ways that are now showing up directly in U.S. inflation expectations. Five-year breakeven rates — the market's forward-looking measure of inflation embedded in Treasury pricing — have climbed to 2.69%, the highest reading since early 2023, according to analysis from Deloitte Insights and Crestwood Advisors. Futures markets have responded accordingly, now pricing a 74.5% probability that the Fed holds rates flat for the entirety of 2026. That is a remarkable figure, and it reflects the trap the central bank finds itself in: energy-driven inflation is pushing prices higher, but the underlying economy is softening in ways that would normally argue for cuts, not stasis. The closure of the Hormuz strait is not a monetary policy problem — it is a geopolitical one — and that distinction is both obvious and, for the Fed, profoundly uncomfortable. Jerome Powell and the FOMC cannot pump more oil or reopen shipping lanes. What they can do is hold the line and hope the supply shock is transitory, a word the institution has learned to use with considerable care after the inflation episode of 2021 and 2022. The risk this time is different in structure if not entirely in kind. Post-pandemic inflation was broad-based and demand-driven in significant part; this shock is narrower in origin but no less persistent in its effects, and it arrives at a moment when consumer sentiment is already fragile and business investment is softening. Rate cuts, which many economists and market participants expected to begin by mid-year, now look remote. That matters for housing, where affordability remains stretched, and for corporate borrowing costs, where higher-for-longer rates are beginning to bite at the margins for leveraged companies. It also matters politically, with the administration watching energy prices feed through into gasoline costs and grocery bills in an election-adjacent environment. The Fed has very few good options, and the resolution of the Hormuz crisis — diplomatic, military, or otherwise — will determine the rate path more than anything the central bank itself decides in the months ahead.

IV.

Preakness Upset: With Golden Tempo Gone, the Triple Crown Race Is Anyone's

The Kentucky Derby winner didn't show, and the Preakness didn't care. Napoleon Solo, guided by jockey Paco Lopez, won the 151st running of the Preakness Stakes at Laurel Park on Saturday in a race that was reshaped entirely by the withdrawal of Golden Tempo, the Derby champion who had arrived as the heavy favorite and the most credible Triple Crown threat the sport had seen in several years. Without Golden Tempo in the field, the race was genuinely open, and Napoleon Solo made the most of the absence, claiming the colt's first win of the 2026 season in front of a crowd that had come expecting a coronation and instead got a wide-open contest. The result means the Triple Crown sequence heads into the Belmont Stakes without a horse holding two legs of the title — a circumstance that simultaneously deflates some of the narrative drama and opens the door for a broader field of contenders. Triple Crown runs are rare partly because they require a horse to be exceptional and partly because they require the exceptional horse to stay healthy through three demanding races in five weeks. Golden Tempo's withdrawal, reported by NPR and NBC Sports without specifying the nature of the issue, preserves the animal's longer-term prospects but leaves the sport without its most compelling through-line heading into June. Napoleon Solo's connections will now face their own decision about whether to take on the Belmont, where the mile-and-a-half distance tests a different set of physical qualities than the Preakness's shorter configuration at Laurel. Lopez, whose ride was described as tactically patient, positioned the horse well through the early fractions and had enough left in the tank to hold off challengers in the stretch. Horse racing tends to generate its most durable stories not from the races where everything goes according to plan but from the disruptions — the upsets, the withdrawals, the unexpected champions — and Napoleon Solo has now written itself into the 2026 Triple Crown story in a way nobody quite anticipated. The Belmont Stakes, and whatever field assembles for it, will carry the weight of resolution.
