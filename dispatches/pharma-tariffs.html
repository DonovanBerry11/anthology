The Pharmacy Bill

May 2026 · Published 9 May 2026, 3:54 PM EST

With 83 days until the first effective date, the mechanics of Trump's pharmaceutical tariffs remain widely misunderstood — coverage has concentrated on the headline 100% rate while the tiered structure underneath it has gone largely unreported.

The April 2 proclamation invoked Section 232 of the Trade Expansion Act of 1962 — the same national security authority used for steel and aluminium — to impose tariffs on patented pharmaceuticals listed in the FDA's Orange Book and Purple Book, along with their active pharmaceutical ingredients and key starting materials. The 100% rate is the default, but it is not the only rate available, and the alternatives are doing substantial policy work.

Companies with Secretary-approved onshoring plans qualify for a 20% rate — a significant reduction, though not a permanent one. The reduced rate reverts to 100% after four years regardless of production progress made. A third tier operates at zero: companies that have entered, or are deemed likely to be eligible to enter, Most Favoured Nation pharmaceutical pricing agreements with the administration pay no Section 232 tariff from 29 September 2026 through January 2029. The MFN tier functions less as a trade instrument than as a domestic drug pricing lever — accept US government pricing terms, or face a 100% import duty on patented products. The White House fact sheet presents this as a supply chain and national security measure; the pricing tier makes clear that drug cost is an equally central objective.

Implementation is staggered. Seventeen named large companies face the first tranche on 31 July — 120 days from the proclamation. All other covered companies follow on 29 September. Country-specific rates apply to allied trading partners: the United Kingdom at 10%, the European Union, Japan, South Korea, and Switzerland at 15%, reflecting pharmaceutical commitments in existing bilateral arrangements. A floating rate mechanism adjusts the Section 232 rate downward where a product already carries a non-zero standard MFN tariff, so that total cumulative duty reaches but does not exceed 100%. Trade counsel are still working through the implications for specific product categories.

What to watch: 31 July is the first compliance deadline. Which of the 17 named companies has secured an approved onshoring plan or MFN pricing agreement — and which faces the full rate — will be the first real test of whether the tariff's incentive architecture is functioning as designed or operating purely as a revenue and pressure instrument.
