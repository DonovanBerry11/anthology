US Fires on Iranian Tankers as Hormuz Talks Stall

May 2026 · Published 10 May 2026, 2:48 AM EST

The United States Navy disabled two Iranian-flagged oil tankers in the Strait of Hormuz on May 8, striking both vessels with precision munitions while Iran has yet to respond to Washington's latest proposal to end a conflict now in its seventieth day.

U.S. Central Command reported that a Navy F/A-18 Super Hornet operating from USS George H.W. Bush struck the smokestacks of the Sea Star III and the Sevda as the two tankers attempted to enter an Iranian port in violation of the U.S. naval blockade. Neither vessel had been transmitting its Automatic Identification System signal for weeks — the Sea Star III for more than a month, the Sevda for 27 days — part of what CENTCOM described as a covert effort to break the blockade. No casualties were reported. President Trump, asked whether the strikes signaled the end of the ceasefire, told reporters the action was a "love tap" and that the month-old ceasefire remained in effect.

The strikes come as negotiators are working toward a preliminary framework to end the conflict. According to Axios, both sides are close to a 14-point memorandum of understanding that would declare an end to hostilities and open a 30-day negotiating window on three issues: reopening the Strait of Hormuz to international shipping, limiting Iran's nuclear program, and lifting U.S. sanctions. Talks are being mediated by Pakistan. Iranian Foreign Ministry spokesperson Esmaeil Baghaei said on Saturday that Tehran was still reviewing Washington's proposal, adding: "We do our own work, we don't pay attention to deadlines or timing." The statement signaled no imminent Iranian answer.

Iranian officials have in recent days sharpened their public framing of the strait's importance. Al Jazeera reported on Saturday that an Iranian official described control of the Strait of Hormuz as a capability comparable in economic impact "to the level of an atomic bomb." The remark came as Tehran continued to push for Hormuz to be addressed first in any agreement — a sequencing demand that Washington has not publicly accepted.

The standoff's toll on energy markets is accumulating. Gulf News reported this week that traffic through the strait has effectively come to a standstill, with oil prices climbing to their highest level of the year.

What to watch: Iran's formal response to the 14-point MOU; Axios reported as of May 6 that both sides were still moving toward the framework, but Tehran had not committed to any response deadline as of Saturday morning.
