China's Legal Shield

May 2026 · Published 9 May 2026, 7:37 AM EST

On 2 May, China's Ministry of Commerce issued a formal prohibition order under its 2021 Blocking Rules, barring Chinese firms from recognising or complying with US sanctions on five domestic oil refineries — the first time Beijing has deployed this legal mechanism in the five years since it was enacted.

The underlying sanctions were imposed by the US Office of Foreign Assets Control as part of the Trump administration's renewed maximum pressure campaign on Iran, which intensified following US and Israeli strikes on Iran's nuclear infrastructure in February. On 24 April, OFAC placed Hengli Petrochemical (Dalian) — China's second-largest independent refinery and one of the largest single buyers of Iranian crude — on the Specially Designated Nationals list. Four Shandong province "teapot" refineries were designated alongside it: Shouguang Luqing Petrochemical, Jincheng Petrochemical Group, Xinhai Chemical Group, and Shengxing Chemical. An OFAC alert on 28 April extended the warning to the teapot sector as a category, signalling that the designations were not isolated but the leading edge of a broader enforcement push.

China's response was unusually formal. The MOFCOM order declares the US SDN listings an unjustified extraterritorial application of American law and prohibits Chinese entities from recognising, enforcing, or complying with them. The practical effect is a bilateral compliance trap: any third-country bank, insurer, or shipping firm that honours the US sanctions risks exposure in China; any that ignores them risks exposure in the United States. Previous Chinese responses to US secondary sanctions have operated through diplomatic statements and informal guidance to state-owned firms. A formal legal instrument is categorically different — it creates obligations, not just signals.

The timing is also notable. Bloomberg reported the MOFCOM order landed the same week senior US and Chinese trade officials were in contact over a possible resumption of bilateral talks. Beijing chose escalation through legal architecture rather than through the diplomatic channel, which suggests the Blocking Rules deployment is not primarily a negotiating gesture — it is a precedent.

What to watch: A wind-down licence OFAC issued for Hengli expires on 24 May. How international banks and shipping firms position themselves in the remaining window — and whether any face direct legal action from either government — will determine whether the Blocking Rules remain a paper shield or become an active compliance frontier.
