Trade Court Strikes Down Trump's Section 122 Tariffs

May 2026 · Published 10 May 2026, 7:14 AM EST

On May 7, a 2-1 panel of the US Court of International Trade struck down the administration's 10% global import surcharge, ruling that President Trump exceeded his statutory authority under Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974.

Proclamation 11012, signed on February 20 and effective February 24 through July 24, 2026, was issued the same day the Supreme Court invalidated the administration's IEEPA-based tariff program. Section 122 allows a president to impose tariffs of up to 15% across all imports for up to 150 days without congressional approval, provided a specific statutory trigger is satisfied: the existence of a "balance of payments deficit."

The majority's ruling turns on what that phrase meant in 1974. According to the court's opinion, Congress used "balance of payments deficit" as a term of art referring to specific historical accounting measures — the liquidity balance, official settlements balance, and basic balance — standard in US international accounts at the time of the statute's drafting. The administration argued that the current account balance and the goods trade deficit satisfied the statutory predicate. The majority disagreed, finding those are different measures, and that the proclamation therefore lacked a valid legal basis. The dissenting judge found the majority's reading of the statutory language too restrictive.

Relief extends to three parties: Burlap & Barrel, a New York-based spice retailer; Basic Fun!, a Florida toy company, both represented by the Liberty Justice Center; and the State of Washington. The court declined to issue a nationwide injunction. For all other US importers, the 10% surcharge remains in effect. The three named parties are entitled to refunds of duties collected since February 24.

The ruling establishes a significant precedent: "balance of payments deficit" under Section 122 cannot be read as equivalent to a trade deficit. An appeal to the US Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit — the standard appellate path from CIT decisions, and the same court that heard the IEEPA challenge — is the expected next step. The tariff's stated expiry of July 24 adds a separate deadline; the litigation may resolve the statutory question before that date arrives.
