Gaza Ceasefire: Seven Months In, the Door Is Still Closed

May 2026 · Published 17 May 2026, 1:28 AM EST

The Envoy's Verdict

Seven months after the October 2025 ceasefire, Board of Peace High Representative Nickolay Mladenov told reporters in Jerusalem on May 13 that "the door to Gaza's future is still closed." His statement, issued after a meeting with Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu, was an unusually candid acknowledgement that the ceasefire — the central diplomatic achievement of the Trump administration's first hundred days on the Middle East — has not produced the post-conflict transition it was designed to enable. Mladenov noted that what has transpired is "not what was promised to the Palestinians, and it is not what they deserve," while also warning that it "does not provide Israel with the security it needs to move forward." The ceasefire holds in the narrow sense — fighting has not resumed — but violations occur daily, some of them serious, and Gaza's civilian population remains trapped in a state of suspended emergency.

The Disarmament Deadlock

The structural obstacle is Hamas's weapons. Mladenov has been unambiguous: Hamas disarmament is "not negotiable" as a precondition for phase two of the Trump administration's 20-point peace plan — the phase that encompasses Israeli troop withdrawal, reconstruction, and the establishment of a new Palestinian governance structure. The Board of Peace issued Hamas an April 11 deadline to accept a framework for the gradual handover of all weapons over an eight-month period. Hamas rejected it. The group's position is that it will not discuss phase two until Israel fully implements phase one — specifically, the delivery of humanitarian aid at the volumes stipulated in the original agreement and the partial withdrawal of Israeli forces. This creates a recursive deadlock: Israel ties implementation of phase one commitments to progress on disarmament, Hamas refuses to discuss disarmament until phase one is complete. Neither party faces an external actor with sufficient leverage to break the loop.

The disarmament demand is also structurally difficult for reasons that go beyond Hamas's immediate political calculations. Disarmament without a framework for Palestinian statehood removes Hamas's primary bargaining asset without offering a political horizon in return. Mladenov acknowledged the problem implicitly, saying Hamas must disarm — not "disappear" — which signals awareness that dissolution alone is not a workable demand. But the gap between what Israel will accept as a political settlement and what Hamas would require to agree to disarm has not narrowed since October.

The Humanitarian Deficit

The humanitarian picture provides the concrete measure of phase one failure. The ceasefire agreement stipulated a minimum of 600 humanitarian aid trucks per day entering Gaza. A scorecard released in April 2026 by five major humanitarian organisations — Oxfam, Save the Children, Norwegian Refugee Council, Danish Refugee Council, and Refugees International — found the ceasefire failing on this metric. During the first two weeks of March, truck deliveries declined by 80 percent from already-insufficient levels. OCHA's April humanitarian situation reports confirm that access restrictions and crossing delays remain systemic. The UN has estimated reconstruction will cost more than $50 billion; little funding has been pledged. Nearly 1.8 million displaced people remain dependent on aid deliveries running at a fraction of stipulated minimums. The Board of Peace's own guidelines link reconstruction progress to Hamas's acceptance of the disarmament framework — meaning the people most immediately harmed by the deadlock are those who had no role in creating it.

The Iran Variable

The strategic context that made the October ceasefire possible has changed in ways that have weakened pressure on both parties. A Chatham House analysis published in May 2026 argues directly that the Iran war — launched in February 2026 — has left Gaza neglected. The conflict consumed the diplomatic bandwidth of the US, the Gulf states, and the European partners who had been pressing for ceasefire implementation. Israel no longer faces meaningful international pressure to fulfil phase one commitments when its security posture is shaped by a concurrent regional conflict. Hamas, which historically received support and direction from Iran, has lost its most important external patron, but this has not increased its flexibility — rather, it has reduced the external actors who might credibly persuade it to accept a disarmament framework. The Board of Peace's credibility as a neutral arbiter is also constrained: Mladenov's May 5 letter suggested the organisation would not hold Israel to ceasefire terms if Hamas refused its disarmament proposal, which Hamas and Arab observers read as institutional bias rather than pragmatic leverage.

What to Watch

The Board of Peace has not set a new deadline since April 11 lapsed without result. The critical indicators are: whether the June Security Council session on the Palestinian question — the successor to the April debate recorded in SC16284 — produces new accountability mechanisms; whether the humanitarian crossing situation stabilises following the partial reopening of Zikim in late April; and whether any Gulf state steps into the diplomatic vacuum the Iran war has created.
