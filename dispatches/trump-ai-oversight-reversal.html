The Reversal

May 2026

The Trump administration is studying an executive order to require mandatory pre-deployment government vetting of advanced AI models — a direct reversal of the deregulatory posture it established sixteen months ago.

The catalyst is Anthropic's Mythos, a frontier AI model capable of identifying decades-old security vulnerabilities in web browsers, software, and critical infrastructure. Axios reported on 5 May that Mythos triggered serious internal concern: in the right hands, the model accelerates defensive patching; in the wrong hands, it gives malicious actors a faster path to identifying exploitable vulnerabilities at scale. The dual-use character of the capability — powerful enough to be useful to defenders, powerful enough to be dangerous in adversarial contexts — appears to have moved the argument inside the White House in ways that years of abstract AI safety advocacy did not.

The policy reversal is stark. In January 2025, one of Trump's first executive orders — "Removing Barriers to American Leadership in Artificial Intelligence" — explicitly dismantled Biden-era AI oversight requirements: mandatory red-teaming for high-risk models, enhanced cybersecurity protocols, and monitoring requirements were all removed. The White House is now considering a framework that Kevin Hassett, director of the National Economic Council, compared to FDA pre-market drug evaluation — requiring that new models be assessed for safety before public release.

Alongside the executive order discussions, on 5 May the Centre for AI Standards and Innovation (CAISI) — NIST's AI evaluation unit, formerly the US AI Safety Institute — announced pre-deployment evaluation agreements with Google DeepMind, Microsoft, and xAI. Anthropic and OpenAI had signed similar agreements under the Biden administration; all five major frontier AI developers are now inside the framework. CAISI has completed more than 40 evaluations to date, including assessments of unreleased models, sometimes provided with reduced safeguards to allow thorough national security testing.

The shift matters not because the policy is settled — the executive order remains under study, not signed — but because of what moved it. The AI safety debate has spent years arguing about existential risks, labour displacement, and algorithmic bias. What appears to have worked inside a deregulation-committed White House is a concrete, near-term security framing: a specific model, a specific capability, a specific threat vector. Whether the administration's response proves durable or reverts when the immediate concern fades is the question the CAISI agreements will, over time, answer.

What to watch: the White House has briefed Anthropic, Google, and OpenAI on the proposed working group structure. Federal News Network reported the order remains in early study. The next signal will be whether the administration circulates draft language for industry comment or moves directly to signature.
