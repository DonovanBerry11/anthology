The Worst of Both Worlds

2026-05-06

The commentary on Trump's economic interventionism has settled into a comfortable frame: this is a departure from American free-market orthodoxy. The United States, which spent four decades moralising about the market-distorting effects of Chinese industrial policy, is now doing industrial policy. The hypocrisy is real and genuinely worth noting. But the framing, which captures the surface contradiction neatly, misses the analytically significant question. The question is not whether the United States is practising state capitalism. It is what kind — and the answer to that question is considerably more disturbing than the hypocrisy.

Consider two equity stakes, both taken by the US government in private companies in the early months of the current administration. In one, the Department of Defense entered a partnership with MP Materials, the dominant American rare earth mining company, to secure domestic critical mineral supply chains — an arrangement involving government investment in exchange for supply commitments and strategic access. In another — reported variously as a golden share arrangement and a structural condition — government equity participation became the mechanism by which a previously blocked foreign acquisition of US Steel was conditionally permitted to proceed. Both involve direct government ownership claims over private productive capacity. Described formally, they look identical. Their logic could not be more different. The first is an industrial policy decision with a coherent strategic rationale: rare earth processing is a genuine national security liability, Chinese firms control approximately 85 percent of global rare earth processing capacity, and securing domestic supply has a defensible, long-horizon justification. The second is a transaction: state power deployed as a bargaining chip, with equity as the consideration paid for regulatory approval. One is state capitalism with a goal. The other is state capitalism as a toll.

The distinction is not merely categorical. It determines what the policy produces. And the current administration's model, on examination, is constituted far more by the second logic than the first — which is why the standard comparison to Chinese industrial strategy or European economic nationalism consistently misses the point.

To understand why the American variant is analytically distinctive, it is worth being precise about what the established models of state capitalism actually are and why, on their own terms, they are internally coherent.

China's model is best understood as strategic state capitalism: a system in which state intervention is oriented by long-horizon national priorities, executed through a combination of directed credit, state-owned enterprises, and strategic equity stakes, and evaluated against industrial outcomes rather than short-term financial returns. The priorities are explicit — semiconductors, electric vehicles, battery technology, rare earth processing, advanced manufacturing — and the institutional apparatus for pursuing them is substantial: development banks, state-owned enterprises, party-directed firms with access to subsidised capital. The model has well-documented failures: overcapacity in steel and solar that has destabilised global markets; inefficient allocation in sectors where strategic logic overrides commercial sense; and the fundamental governance problem that the same party that sets industrial priorities also evaluates their performance. But the internal logic is coherent. The state intervenes because it has identified strategic objectives that private capital, operating on commercial time horizons, will not pursue at the required scale. The intervention is designed to produce specific industrial outcomes. Whether it succeeds or fails, the rationale is intelligible.

Europe's industrial policy is structured differently and justified on different grounds. The European model is not primarily strategic — it does not identify geopolitical targets and direct state resources toward them — but regulatory and socially embedded. Industrial policy in Germany, France, and the EU at large operates through a framework of state aid rules, competition policy, and democratic accountability that constrains how governments can intervene and requires public justification of why. Subsidies to semiconductor manufacturers under the EU Chips Act, investments in battery supply chains through the European Battery Alliance, German state support for energy transition industries: these are contestable, contested in courts, subject to parliamentary scrutiny, and structured by legal rules that apply regardless of the political relationship between the recipient firm and the government of the day. The model is slower and more friction-laden than the Chinese version precisely because accountability mechanisms slow things down. But the friction is a feature: it is what prevents the policy from being captured by the firms that benefit from it. The objection that democratic accountability is an unaffordable luxury in fast-moving strategic domains — semiconductors, rare earths, AI infrastructure — has genuine force. What the current American period reveals, however, is that the alternative to democratic accountability is not strategic decisiveness. It is arbitrariness. The choice is not between accountability and speed; it is between accountability and caprice.

These two models share the label of state capitalism, but their internal operating logics are almost entirely different. The Chinese model optimises for strategic outcome at the cost of democratic accountability. The European model optimises for accountability at the cost of strategic speed. Both have compensating mechanisms for their respective deficiencies. Both can be internally evaluated by whether they are actually achieving what they say they are trying to achieve. The comparison is analytically useful not because the models are equivalent but because they establish the parameters within which state capitalism can be justified on its own terms.

The Trump administration's economic interventionism does not fit either parameter. And this is not simply because it is American, or because its institutional context differs from China's or Europe's. It is because the governing logic of the intervention is different in kind.

The Chinese model requires a bureaucratic apparatus capable of identifying strategic priorities and allocating resources toward them over time horizons that extend beyond electoral cycles. The Trump model has no such apparatus and shows no interest in building one. The sovereign wealth fund announced by executive order in February 2025 — which would, in theory, provide the institutional vehicle for strategic state equity — remains without a funding mechanism, a mandate, or an operational structure as of mid-2026, more than a year after its announcement. The CHIPS Act, which does represent genuinely strategic industrial policy, was legislated under a previous administration and has survived largely because its institutional structure is sufficiently embedded to resist political redirection. Where the current administration has generated new industrial policy, the pattern is not strategic prioritisation — a list of sectors where state investment is justified by long-horizon national interest — but case-by-case deal-making in which the terms are negotiated directly between firms and the executive, and the relevant variable is not industrial merit but relationship.

This is the pattern that Eurasia Group was gesturing at without quite naming when its January 2026 Top Risks report placed "state capitalism with American characteristics" at the head of its geopolitical concerns for the year — identifying the discretionary, deal-by-deal character of the interventionism as its distinguishing and most dangerous feature. The problem is not the size of government intervention but its structure. Tariff exemptions negotiated firm-by-firm, with outcomes that correlate observably with political alignment. Regulatory approvals conditioned on equity stakes that transfer value to the state — or to state-adjacent entities — as the price of doing business. The systematic use of trade policy as a bilateral negotiating tool in which individual companies, rather than sectors or supply chains, are the unit of engagement. Foreign Policy's 2025 investigation into the administration's direct and quasi-equity positions across more than a dozen companies captures the pattern at the level of the portfolio, but the more significant point is structural: what is being built is not an industrial policy but a relationship economy — one in which access to the market, regulatory certainty, and freedom from trade penalties is determined by the quality of the firm's political relationship with the executive.

The political science literature has a name for this configuration. Max Weber distinguished between market capitalism — in which profits derive from exchange in competitive markets — and political capitalism, in which profits derive from the exploitation of political opportunities and relationships with power. Branko Milanovic, updating this framework for contemporary analysis, uses political capitalism to describe systems in which a politically connected elite controls the state and uses it to generate returns for affiliated economic actors, with the key characteristic that the boundary between state and private power is deliberately blurred rather than institutionally maintained. Trump's model fits this description with uncomfortable precision. The defining characteristic is not the scale of state intervention but its discretionary, personalised, relationship-dependent character. In a system of political capitalism, the relevant strategic question for a firm is not "what do I need to produce?" but "what do I need to do to secure a good relationship with the people who control access?" These are categorically different incentive structures, and they produce categorically different economic outcomes.

There is a genuine complication here that needs to be addressed directly rather than minimised. Some of what the current administration is doing has a legitimate strategic logic that the political capitalism frame, applied too bluntly, would obscure. The rare earth partnership with MP Materials, whatever its implementation deficiencies, addresses a real supply chain vulnerability. The sustained focus on semiconductor domestic capacity, even where it amounts to taking credit for the institutional momentum of the previous administration's CHIPS Act investments, reflects a genuine strategic priority. The administration's posture toward Chinese technology firms and the enforcement of export controls on advanced semiconductors — continued and in some respects strengthened from prior policy — has a coherent strategic rationale even where the specific decisions are contested.

The complication is not that no strategic logic exists. It is that strategic logic cannot survive being embedded in a patronage system. Strategic industrial policy requires a specific institutional property: insulation from capture by the firms that benefit from it. The reason this insulation is necessary is well understood from the political economy of industrial policy: if the firms receiving state support can influence which firms receive state support, the selection mechanism shifts from strategic merit to political relationship, and the policy ceases to function as industrial strategy. It becomes instead a subsidy programme for politically connected incumbents. This is the dynamic that China's bureaucratic apparatus, for all its other failures, is partially designed to resist — the separation between party-directed strategic priority-setting and the commercial operations of state-adjacent firms, imperfect as it is, provides some degree of insulation. European industrial policy provides insulation through legal constraint: the state aid rules that apply regardless of which firm happens to have a good relationship with the government of the day.

The Trump model provides neither insulation. The same discretionary executive authority that can grant tariff exemptions to politically aligned firms can deny them to firms that have not maintained the relationship. The same equity stake mechanism that might in theory secure a strategic supply chain can also be used to extract value from firms seeking regulatory approval. A strategic industrial programme cannot function predictably within this system because the decisions that determine which investments are protected, which supply chains are prioritised, and which firms receive competitive advantages are made through the same discretionary channel as purely transactional deal-making. The administration's tariff exemption decisions illustrate the mechanism precisely. The industrial logic — domestic manufacturing, supply chain resilience — that might justify a tariff regime is present at the level of the stated goal. But the decision process that determines which firms receive relief is governed by the quality of the relationship: which executives made domestic investment announcements, which companies cultivated proximity to the executive, which deals were structured to produce visible political wins. The strategic objective survives the translation into rhetoric; the strategic mechanism — impersonal, merit-based, insulated from political pressure — does not. Capital responds to incentives, and the incentive the current model creates is not to invest in strategically important sectors but to invest in politically important relationships. The MP Materials partnership and the US Steel arrangement look different strategically; from the perspective of the firm calculating how to operate in this environment, they demonstrate the same lesson — that the relationship is the asset.

The longer-run implications of this model are more consequential than the near-term policy failures, which have attracted most of the commentary. The observation that tariff policy is incoherent, that the sovereign wealth fund is a vehicle without a purpose, that tariff exemption negotiations create uncertainty that undermines the investment they are supposed to promote — these are correct, and they matter. But they treat the dysfunction as a bug in what is otherwise intended to be an industrial policy. The more analytically significant point is that these outcomes are structurally predictable given the model's operating logic. A system in which political relationships govern economic access will generate precisely this pattern: exemption decisions that vary by relationship rather than industrial merit; a wealth fund that cannot acquire an impersonal mandate without constraining the discretion that makes it politically valuable; tariff negotiations that produce uncertainty because their purpose is to sustain leverage rather than to set stable rules. The incoherence is not a failure of execution. It is the characteristic form that a relationship economy takes in practice.

What political capitalism produces, as a structural matter, is the fusion of state power and private capital through personal networks rather than institutional rules. The firms that emerge from this system as winners are not those that have invested most productively in strategic sectors; they are those that have most successfully cultivated the relationship. Over time, this creates a political economy in which competitive position correlates with political access — in which the boundary between the state and the ownership class that benefits from state favour is progressively dissolved. This is not a description of Chinese strategic state capitalism or European democratic industrial policy. It is a description of the political economy of oligarchy.

The comparison that the current moment actually invites is not to China or to Germany. It is to post-Soviet resource states — to the configuration that emerged in Russia in the 1990s, and in various forms across the post-communist world, in which the privatisation of state assets and the state's continued use of regulatory and legal power created a class of politically connected beneficiaries whose wealth depended on continued political access rather than productive investment. The analogy is imperfect and should not be pressed to the level of institutional outcomes: American institutions are more robust, the rule of law is more entrenched, and the structural preconditions differ in ways that matter significantly for how far the dynamic can develop. What the comparison captures is the incentive logic at the level of the firm: in both configurations, the question "how do I secure my position?" is answered not by "produce better" but by "cultivate the relationship." That shift tends to produce a common structural consequence — the progressive alignment of competitive advantage with political access — but how far it develops, and whether countervailing institutions arrest it, depends precisely on the institutional robustness the analogy cannot simply set aside.

The debate about whether Trump's economic interventionism will work — whether tariffs will generate the promised reshoring, whether the sovereign wealth fund will eventually be capitalised, whether American industrial policy can build the semiconductor capacity it aspires to — is the wrong debate. These are questions about the delivery of stated industrial objectives, and they matter. But they implicitly accept that the model's stated objectives are its actual objectives.

The right question is structural: what kind of political economy does this model produce, independent of whether it delivers its stated industrial goals? And the answer, based on the operating logic rather than the rhetorical framing, is one in which the boundary between state power and private wealth is governed by personal relationship rather than institutional rule — in which the firms that thrive are those that have invested in the relationship economy, and in which the accumulation of both economic and political power tends toward the same network of actors.

This is not state capitalism in the Chinese or European sense. It lacks the strategic coherence of the former and the democratic accountability of the latter. It combines the inefficiency risks of state intervention — the misallocation that follows when political decisions substitute for commercial ones — with the concentration risks of unaccountable private power — the entrenchment that follows when access to markets and regulatory certainty depends on maintaining the relationship. It is the worst of both worlds in a precise and non-rhetorical sense: it achieves the downsides of each model without the compensating benefits that make those models, however imperfect, internally defensible.

What it builds, in the meantime, is not a more competitive American economy. It is a political economy increasingly difficult to unwind — not because its industrial achievements will create path dependencies, but because the network of interests it creates around the relationship between state power and private capital will resist disruption regardless of electoral outcomes. Policies can be reversed. Oligarchic structures are considerably harder to dismantle.
