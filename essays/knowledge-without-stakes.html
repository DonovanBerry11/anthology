Knowledge Without Stakes

2026-05-04

There is a peculiarity in the way we have come to discuss the epistemic status of large language models. The debate tends to orbit the question of whether these systems "really" understand anything — whether the fluency of their outputs corresponds to some inner grasp of meaning, or whether it is, as the dismissive formulation goes, mere statistical pattern-matching dressed up as thought. This is not an uninteresting question, but it has a way of drawing attention away from something more consequential: not what kind of thing an LLM is, but what kind of knowledge it produces, and whether the structures that make human knowledge trustworthy — insofar as it is trustworthy — have any analogue in these systems at all.

The diagnostic is stark. When a scientist publishes a fabricated result, the correction mechanism is social and consequential: retraction, loss of standing, the professional consequences that attach to a researcher who cannot be trusted. When a large language model produces a hallucinated citation — a plausible-sounding paper that does not exist, attributed to a real author who never wrote it — no mechanism of this kind engages. The model bears no consequence. It will produce the same class of error in its next session with the same equanimity with which it produces accurate claims. The asymmetry is stark, and it points toward something structural. The question is what, precisely, is structurally absent.

The conventional answer reaches for social embeddedness. Human knowledge production is grounded in social structures; LLMs, at least in their more autonomous configurations, are not. This answer captures something real, but it requires considerably more precision before it can do the analytical work being asked of it.

The social and cultural grounding of human cognition is not merely a sociological contingency — it is, in the anthropologist Joseph Henrich's argument, the primary explanation for why human beings are cognitively distinctive at all. In The Secret of Our Success, Henrich makes the case that what separates human from non-human cognition is less individual intelligence than the capacity for cumulative cultural learning: the ability to absorb, recombine, and transmit acquired knowledge across generations, building on what predecessors established rather than beginning again from individual experience. Human beings are not, in Henrich's account, remarkable because any one of us is particularly clever. We are remarkable because our social structures allow for a ratcheting accumulation of knowledge, technique, and normative convention that no individual could generate alone and that no single lifetime could replicate. The cognitive tools we deploy — including the mental models through which we carve up and reason about the world — are neither purely biological endowments nor purely cultural inheritances, but products of what Henrich and colleagues describe as gene-culture co-evolution: cultural practices reshape the environments that drive biological selection, and the biological capacities that result in turn enable further cultural development. The particular features of human social cognition — our sensitivity to status and norm violation, our tendency toward conformist transmission, our capacity to learn from and coordinate with strangers — were, on this account, shaped by selection pressures that operated within cultural environments, over timescales that dwarf any individual learning process.

This means that "social grounding" names something genuinely important about human knowledge production. But it does not name something uniformly positive. The cumulative cultural learning that Henrich describes is a mechanism with an output, and the quality of that output depends on the quality of the feedback loops embedded in the cultural system. Evolutionary calibration is fitness calibration, not accuracy calibration — and fitness, in the relevant sense, tracks survival and reproductive success, not truth. Much of what evolutionary selection rewarded in our cognitive ancestors — rapid threat assessment, status sensitivity, in-group solidarity, the tendency to read agency into ambiguous events — becomes a systematic liability when applied to statistical reasoning, long-run risk assessment, or the behaviour of complex systems that extend far beyond ancestral environments. The history of modern science is, in substantial measure, a history of disciplined effort to correct the errors that evolutionarily shaped cognition reliably produces: our intuitions about probability, causation, and scale are deeply unreliable in precisely the domains where contemporary knowledge production is most consequential.

What saves human knowledge production from the full implications of these distortions is not social embedding per se, but a more specific feature of it: the possibility of feedback between belief and a reality that does not accommodate itself to preference. We act on our models of the world; the world responds; the response, when we are able and willing to attend to it, disciplines what we can continue to believe. This loop is irregular, often very slow, vulnerable to motivated reasoning and institutional inertia. But it exists. And its existence is what creates the structural possibility of genuine correction — the mechanism by which wrong beliefs, over time and with friction, can be forced out by encounter with reality.

What, then, do large language models inherit from this system? More than the "purely probabilistic" characterisation that often appears in discussions of this kind would suggest. The probabilistic relationships that a language model learns from training on human-generated text are not arbitrary statistical regularities. They are regularities in text produced by people trying, imperfectly and with variable success, to describe, argue about, coordinate around, and make sense of the world. The co-occurrence patterns in that text carry semantic structure, normative commitment, and implicit causal theory. When an LLM learns something about how certain concepts cluster, it is learning something about how human communities of practice have organised their understanding of reality. The model inherits, in a compressed and imprecise form, the outputs of the cumulative cultural learning process that Henrich describes.

This inheritance is not nothing. It is, in fact, quite remarkable — and it is what makes LLM outputs so cognitively compelling, so usable, so apparently trustworthy. But it is an inheritance of products, not of process. What the cultural transmission systems that underpin human knowledge production actually do is not just accumulate outputs; they maintain and operate the feedback loops through which outputs are tested, revised, discarded, and refined. Peer review, replication norms, the possibility of public falsification, the professional consequences attached to producing claims that do not survive scrutiny — these are the institutional forms through which the feedback loop between knowledge and reality is kept operative. An LLM trained on the scientific literature absorbs the results of this process but is not subject to its discipline. It learns what scientists say without being embedded in the conditions under which saying false things has consequences.

This suggests that the correct reframing of the question is not about social embedding but about accountability — a more specific and more tractable concept. Accountability, in the sense relevant here, is the arrangement by which those who produce claims about the world bear some relationship to the consequences of those claims being wrong. It is the condition under which skin in the game, as Nassim Taleb has usefully if bluntly named it, applies: the person making the assertion stands to lose something if the assertion fails.

The distinction between social embedding and accountability matters because the two come apart in instructive ways. Consider several episodes in which socially embedded knowledge production — produced by credentialled professionals operating within established institutions — generated and sustained serious, sometimes catastrophic error. Eugenics was not a fringe enthusiasm; it was endorsed by the most respected scientific institutions of the early twentieth century, developed by statisticians and biologists of genuine achievement, and sustained by exactly the kind of social consensus that the "embeddedness" account would predict should confer epistemic authority. The decades-long medical consensus that peptic ulcers were caused by stress resisted not only the bacterial hypothesis but the people who advanced it, despite accumulating evidence, because the social structures organising that consensus were oriented toward the maintenance of orthodoxy rather than toward encounter with reality. These errors were eventually corrected — but not by social embeddedness. They were corrected by the kind of encounter with unforgiving evidence that could no longer be institutionally managed: predictions that failed to materialise, treatments that did not work, experiments that could not be explained away.

What the eventual corrections had that the sustained errors lacked was not better social grounding. Both the errors and their corrections were produced by socially embedded communities. The difference was in the accountability structure — whether the loop between knowledge production, action, and consequence was allowed to operate, or was blocked by institutional arrangements that insulated beliefs from the feedback of reality.

It might seem to follow from the argument so far that autonomous LLM knowledge production — systems operating without ongoing human involvement — is best understood as simply asocial: cut loose from the social structures that ground and discipline human knowledge, and therefore operating in a categorically different epistemic register. The intuition has a certain logic to it. But it is inaccurate in a way that matters practically, and the inaccuracy points toward something more troubling than the asocial picture suggests. An LLM agent operating without ongoing human oversight is not genuinely operating outside social structures. The reasoning patterns it deploys, the conceptual categories it employs, the normative commitments it reflects — all of these are inheritances from training data produced within the dense social structures of human culture and institution. Its outputs will look, at the level of form and surface, like the outputs of socially-grounded knowledge production. They will make claims in recognisably human ways, draw on recognisably human evidence conventions, and present conclusions in formats that carry the familiar markers of epistemic authority.

The appropriate characterisation is not asocial but pseudosocial: the production of outputs that bear the form of accountable knowledge without the accountability structure that makes such knowledge trustworthy. The distinction is not merely terminological. Genuinely asocial knowledge production — outputs that failed to conform to human epistemic norms, that were recognisably alien in their structure or their claims — would trigger the appropriate sceptical response. Pseudosocial outputs do not. They circulate within human epistemic communities carrying the surface authority of the knowledge they structurally resemble. They get cited, extended, built upon, integrated into the working assumptions of researchers and practitioners and institutions — without activating the alarms that would fire if the alien origin of the knowledge were more visible. The risk is not that autonomous LLM agents will generate claims nobody believes. It is that they will generate claims everybody accepts.

This is to say that the most dangerous feature of unconstrained LLM knowledge production is not its distance from human social grounding. It is its proximity.

The intermediate case — human beings working in conjunction with LLM systems — is often presented as straightforwardly safer, on the grounds that human social embedding provides a corrective check on LLM outputs. This is correct as far as it goes, but it does not go far enough. The safety of the hybrid configuration does not derive primarily from the fact that a human is present in the loop. It derives from the fact that the human, unlike the LLM, bears consequences for acting on the outputs. When a researcher builds published work on LLM-assisted literature synthesis, accountability for the resulting claims rests with the researcher. When a clinician uses LLM-assisted diagnostic support and the diagnosis is wrong, it is the clinician who faces professional and legal consequence. The human participant is not simply a quality-control function — they are the locus of accountability that keeps the feedback loop operative.

This framing carries an important implication that the standard picture of "human oversight" tends to obscure. The accountability that makes the hybrid configuration epistemically safer is not structural — it is not guaranteed by the formal presence of a human reviewer. It is behavioural, and it degrades. As LLM systems become more capable and human review becomes more perfunctory — as the volume of output increases and the cognitive cost of genuine scrutiny rises — the practical distance between the hybrid and the autonomous case narrows, regardless of the formal distinction between them. A human who reviews LLM outputs without genuine engagement, who signs off on claims they have not actually tested, who bears nominal rather than real accountability for the consequences of being wrong: this human is not maintaining the feedback loop. They are providing its appearance while allowing its substance to erode.

Two versions of the question that runs through this analysis tend to get conflated in public discussion of AI and knowledge production. The first is technical: can LLMs be made more accurate, more reliable, less prone to hallucination? This question is important and is receiving considerable resources. The second is institutional: can we construct accountability structures around LLM knowledge production that reproduce the epistemic pressures through which human knowledge production — fitfully, slowly, with enormous friction — manages to be corrected by reality? This question is receiving far less attention, and it is the harder one.

The institutional challenge is not simply a matter of attaching human reviewers to LLM outputs, or of building better evaluation frameworks, or of requiring citations. These things matter but they do not address the underlying structure. What is required is the kind of thing that took centuries to build in the domains of scientific and institutional knowledge production: arrangements in which the production of false claims generates consequences for those who produce them, in which errors have costs that feed back into the incentive structures of knowledge producers, in which the gap between what is claimed and what is true is not merely regrettable but professionally and practically expensive.

Building these arrangements for systems that produce knowledge at scale, without individual authorship, without clear lines of consequence, and without the kind of social embeddedness through which accountability has historically been enforced — this is a problem of institutional design for which there is no established template. The technical work of building more capable models is proceeding at a pace that outstrips it considerably. The gap between the pseudosocial surface of autonomous LLM outputs and the absence of the accountability structure that would make such outputs genuinely trustworthy is not a gap that better engineering will close. It is the central problem of the epistemology of artificial intelligence — and the institutional design question it poses, as distinct from the technical accuracy question, remains the less addressed of the two.
