The Ownership Problem

2026-05-05

Most commentary on artificial intelligence and the economy reaches for one of three registers. The first is techno-optimism: productivity gains will expand the economic pie, and historical precedent suggests that new technologies create more jobs than they destroy. The second is labour market anxiety: AI will displace workers at a scale and speed that previous technological transitions did not, and the social costs will be severe. The third is distributional concern: AI will widen the gap between winners and losers, and some form of compensatory policy will be required. Each of these framings captures something real. None of them is sufficient. The more fundamental problem — which the dominant discourse has been slow to address directly — is that we do not yet have an analytical framework adequate to hold the structural, political, and geopolitical dimensions of what is happening simultaneously. Without such a framework, predictive capacity is necessarily weak. We are reasoning about a transformation of potentially historic scale with tools built for different problems.

This is not an argument for paralysis. It is an argument for epistemic honesty about what we know, what we are guessing, and what we cannot yet see. What follows attempts a better framing — not a complete one, but one that at least identifies the right question. That question is not, as the dominant discourse assumes, how society will adjust to labour displacement. It is who will own the productive capacity of the AI economy. This is a question about property rights and the structure of capital ownership, and the political economy of the coming decades will be defined by how it is answered — or evaded.

The K-shaped economy is by now a familiar description, but its structural logic deserves more attention than it typically receives. The term entered circulation during the COVID-19 recovery to describe a bifurcated economic trajectory: asset owners recovering strongly while wage-dependent workers stagnated or declined. The observation was accurate, but the framing was misleading insofar as it implied that the K-shape was a product of the pandemic disruption. In fact, the divergence it describes has been the defining feature of developed-economy political economy for at least four decades, with roots in the structural shift of the 1980s toward capital-light, financialised economic models and the secular decline of labour's share of national income.

The mechanism is not complicated. Asset ownership — equity, real estate, financial instruments — generates returns that compound over time. Those returns are both a direct income source and, through the wealth effect, a driver of consumption spending that is largely independent of labour market conditions. For the asset-owning segment of the population, economic wellbeing is increasingly decoupled from wages. For the asset-poor segment — those whose consumption depends primarily on labour income — the dynamics are almost entirely inverted: they are exposed to labour market conditions, capture little or none of the return to capital, and have no buffer when wage income is disrupted.

This bifurcation is not new. What is new is the accelerant that AI-driven productivity represents for it. The conventional framing — AI as a labour market disruption requiring income support — mistakes the surface phenomenon for the structural one. Labour displacement is the visible symptom. The underlying event is a further shift in the balance between labour and capital as claimants on economic output.

The logic of this shift is straightforward, even if its magnitude and pace are uncertain. AI-driven automation reduces the labour input required to produce a given unit of output. Reduced labour costs translate directly into expanded profit margins. Expanded profit margins increase returns to equity holders. The distribution of those gains follows the distribution of equity ownership — which is to say, they accrue heavily to those already at the top of the K. The asset-poor wage-earner faces a double exposure: their labour income is threatened by the same technological transition that is generating returns for the asset class they do not own.

There is, however, a complication that the standard distributional analysis tends to flatten: the position of knowledge workers. The class of workers most immediately susceptible to large language model (LLM)-driven displacement — analysts, lawyers, researchers, writers, programmers, consultants — overlaps substantially with the class of workers who hold meaningful financial assets. A software engineer with significant equity compensation, a management consultant with a diversified retirement portfolio, a lawyer with stock holdings accumulated over a career — these workers face a genuine erosion of their labour market power while simultaneously holding claims on the capital appreciation that AI is generating. They are, in a specific and limited sense, partially hedged against their own displacement.

This creates a fracture within the category of labour that complicates class analysis. It is not that knowledge workers are unaffected by AI — their labour is devalued, their professional leverage diminished, their career trajectories destabilised. It is that the upper strata of knowledge workers occupy a hybrid position: labour threatened, capital partially compensated. This hybrid position will likely generate distinctive political responses — neither the straightforward labour solidarity of workers with no asset exposure, nor the straightforward capital-owner interest in unconstrained AI deployment. The politics of this group is likely to be incoherent and unstable, reflecting a genuinely contradictory material position.

The more structurally significant group is the one that receives far less analytical attention: the asset-poor knowledge worker and the broader population of wage-dependent households for whom neither their labour nor their capital position provides protection from the distributional consequences of AI. For this group, the relevant question is not income replacement but ownership access — and the current policy discourse has not caught up with that distinction.

The received policy response to AI-driven labour displacement is some version of Universal Basic Income: a guaranteed income floor to cushion the loss of wage income. UBI is not without merit as a transitional measure, but as a structural response to the ownership dynamics at stake it addresses the wrong problem. The issue is not that displaced workers will lack income — though they may. The issue is that the productive capacity generating that income will be owned by a narrow slice of the population, and that the mechanism by which UBI would be funded (taxation of capital or income) implies an ongoing political battle over redistribution that has historically proven easier to win in theory than in practice.

The more coherent structural response is universal equity ownership: ensuring that households across the income distribution hold meaningful claims on the financial assets that benefit from AI-driven productivity gains. This is less radical than it sounds. The institutional infrastructure for it — defined contribution retirement accounts, index funds, broad-based equity vehicles — already exists. The 401k system and the secular rise of passive indexing represent, in embryonic and inadequate form, exactly this kind of ownership broadening. The policy task is to extend, deepen, and mandate the model rather than treat it as a supplementary benefit for those with the income to participate voluntarily.

There is, however, a tension within this thesis that needs to be named rather than papered over. Passive equity ownership in a market increasingly concentrated in a small number of AI-dominant firms is technically broad ownership of narrowly concentrated productive capacity. When the top ten constituents of the S&P 500 account for a disproportionate share of its market capitalisation, and when those constituents are disproportionately AI-infrastructure and platform companies, then "owning the index" does not mean owning a diversified slice of the productive economy. It means owning a leveraged bet on the continued dominance of a small number of firms. Universal ownership of this kind distributes the financial claim without distributing the underlying productive power. It provides a ticket on a train whose route and destination remain determined by the same concentrated ownership class.

A genuine universal equity ownership regime would need to do more than broaden access to existing financial products. It would need to address the concentration of productive assets directly — through mechanisms like social wealth funds, mandatory equity-sharing schemes, or structured public stakes in AI infrastructure. Norway's sovereign wealth fund, capitalised by resource extraction and invested broadly, offers one institutional template. The political economy of building such structures in the United States — where property rights are strongly defended and the policy process is itself heavily influenced by capital owners — is formidable. But it is the correct frame for the problem.

The ownership question, then, is the structurally central one. But the trajectory through which it gets answered — or deferred — runs through a set of real-world frictions that complicate the analysis significantly. These frictions are not independent of each other, and they do not operate at a single level. Before examining how they interact, it is worth being precise about what each of them is, because the current discourse tends to conflate categories that have different logics and different policy implications.

The most visible friction is social and political resistance to AI-driven concentration itself. Anti-data centre movements at local and state legislative levels, organised opposition to AI deployment in public services, growing discomfort with the consolidation of computational infrastructure in the hands of a small number of private actors — these are early expressions of what Karl Polanyi's framework would recognise as the double movement: the counter-mobilisation of society against the disembedding effects of market expansion. Polanyi's analysis of nineteenth-century industrial capitalism described how the commodification of land, labour, and money produced dislocations that eventually generated political demands for protection. The AI moment has the same structure. Democratic pushback against data centre development in communities that bear the costs — noise, heat, grid load, water consumption — while capturing none of the returns is not irrational or sentimental. It is the articulation of a legitimate distributional grievance through the political channels available at the local level.

This democratic resistance translates, at least partially, into responsive regulation. AI restrictions adopted in public sector contexts, legislative pressure on healthcare and financial services deployment, state-level data governance — these are not primarily technocratic determinations. They are politically mediated expressions of the same double movement, operating through institutional channels rather than street-level mobilisation. The boundary between social backlash and democratically-responsive regulation is porous; they are phases of the same underlying dynamic, not separate phenomena. Treating them as distinct risks miscounting the forces at work.

A second category of regulatory friction operates on a different logic entirely. Compliance frameworks built into independent agency mandates — financial regulation developed over decades, healthcare oversight structures, legal liability regimes — were not designed in response to AI and are not primarily driven by democratic pressure about AI. They are the friction of existing institutional architecture encountering a new capability. This friction is not politically responsive in any direct sense: it reflects prior legislative decisions, court interpretations, and agency rulemaking that now apply to AI deployment regardless of whether the public or elected officials have formed views on the matter. Its distributional consequences are asymmetric in a specific way: large established actors can absorb compliance costs that new entrants cannot, which tends to entrench existing ownership concentration rather than challenge it. This technocratic friction and democratically-responsive regulation are both real, but they pull in different directions and require different analytical treatment. Collapsing them into a single "regulatory constraint" obscures more than it reveals.

The third friction is geopolitical. The productive capacity of the AI economy rests on a globally integrated supply chain whose fragility has become increasingly legible. Semiconductor fabrication is concentrated to an extraordinary degree: TSMC's leading-edge capacity represents the dominant dependency for global AI infrastructure, fabricating the vast majority of the most advanced chips on which frontier AI systems run, and the geopolitical precarity of that concentration — given Taiwan's strategic position — has no ready solution on any near-term horizon. The critical industrial inputs on which this infrastructure depends are similarly concentrated: rare earth minerals for semiconductor processing, specialty industrial gases — neon in particular, a significant proportion of whose supply runs through geopolitically contested producers — and energy commodities whose pricing and availability are embedded in a global system that geopolitical fragmentation is making progressively less reliable.

The domestic grid capacity deficit that large-scale AI deployment is exposing belongs in this analysis, but not straightforwardly as a geopolitical constraint. It is primarily a domestic industrial policy failure — the result of sustained underinvestment in electrical infrastructure that state-directed economies do not face in the same form. China's industrial policy apparatus can direct capital toward grid expansion as a strategic priority in a way that the American policy environment, shaped by fragmented regulatory jurisdiction and private utility structures, cannot. The failure is domestic in origin; its implications are geopolitical in consequence. A country whose grid cannot support its planned AI investment operates at a structural disadvantage relative to one whose state can clear that constraint by directive. Misclassifying this as a geopolitical constraint rather than a domestic policy failure risks displacing the appropriate remedies — which are different for each diagnosis.

What is notable about these frictions is that they do not operate independently of the ownership dynamics they nominally constrain. They interact with each other, and with the structural concentration they encounter, in ways that frequently produce outcomes contrary to their apparent intent.

Polanyian backlash generates democratic pressure for regulatory response; democratically-responsive regulation increases friction on AI deployment broadly, but does not necessarily target ownership concentration specifically. The same political environment that produces anti-data centre movements also generates pressure for domestic semiconductor policy and grid investment — creating new state-adjacent concentrations of productive capacity whose relationship to distributed ownership is, at best, ambiguous. Technocratic regulatory friction, operating on its own institutional logic, advantages incumbents over entrants and may further concentrate ownership rather than challenging it — producing outcomes that align with capital concentration even when the regulations were designed for other purposes. Geopolitical friction in semiconductor supply chains generates pressure for domestic substitution; domestic substitution through state-directed industrial policy concentrates productive capacity in a small number of strategically designated actors, which does not obviously improve the ownership distribution problem.

The feedback loops between these levels are neither stable nor linear. A policy response designed at one level — income support for displaced labour, deployment restrictions on high-risk AI applications, domestic semiconductor manufacturing — will generate effects at other levels that the policy was not designed to address. Income support does not touch the ownership structure. Deployment restrictions slow the pace of returns to capital but do not alter who captures them when they arrive. Domestic semiconductor manufacturing, absent deliberate ownership structure design, recreates in the public-private hybrid sector the same concentration it was meant to reduce elsewhere.

It is precisely this interdependence that makes tractable framing so difficult. The forces at play operate at different levels of analysis — firm, sector, national economy, geopolitical system — and interact through mechanisms that are not fully understood. The current discourse tends toward single-level analysis: labour economists modelling displacement effects, geopolitical analysts tracking semiconductor competition, political scientists studying backlash movements, without integration across levels. The absence is not merely academic. If policymakers are working from an inadequate map — one that captures some features of the terrain while missing the interdependencies — they will systematically misspecify the intervention. Income support programmes designed for a labour displacement problem will be inadequate to an ownership concentration problem. Regulatory frameworks designed to manage AI risk in individual deployment contexts will not address the systemic distributional consequences of AI-driven capital accumulation.

What, then, can be said with reasonable confidence, and what cannot?

With reasonable confidence: AI-driven productivity is structurally deepening the K-shaped economy by shifting returns from labour to capital, and the correct policy frame for this dynamic is ownership structure, not income support. The question of who holds claims on the productive capacity of the AI economy is prior to the question of how to compensate those displaced from it. The constraints and frictions described above are real and will shape the trajectory, but none of them individually is likely to reverse the structural direction — and their interactions may produce outcomes that entrench rather than challenge ownership concentration.

Without reasonable confidence: how the interaction of Polanyian backlash, geopolitical fragmentation, and institutional friction will play out over what timeline; whether the political conditions for a genuine universal equity ownership programme exist or can be created; whether the concentration paradox in passive indexing will be addressed or will entrench a new form of nominally distributed but structurally concentrated ownership; whether knowledge workers' hybrid position will generate politically coherent responses or will dissolve into the broader asset-owner interest as their equity holdings appreciate.

The ownership problem is the central political-economic question of the AI era. It is also a question whose resolution we cannot currently predict, because the framework required to reason about it rigorously does not yet exist in integrated form. Building that framework — one capable of holding the domestic distributional, political, geopolitical, and institutional dimensions simultaneously without collapsing the complexity — is the prior intellectual task. The essays, policy papers, and political programmes that skip this step in favour of confident prescription are not wrong to be concerned about what is coming. They are wrong to be confident about what it will look like.
