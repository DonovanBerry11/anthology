The Bluff That Held, The Logic That Didn't

May 2026

On the first anniversary of Operation Sindoor, the dominant register in Indian strategic commentary is triumphalist. Vice Admiral A.N. Pramod, speaking at the anniversary ceremony, described India as having "called Pakistan's nuclear bluff." The phrase has been taken up across defence journals and newspaper editorials as if it captures the essential meaning of what happened in May 2025: India struck deep into Pakistani territory, Pakistan's nuclear arsenal did not fire, the deterrence order held, and the lesson is clear. India has demonstrated it can conduct significant conventional punishment without triggering escalation to the nuclear threshold. Pakistan's nuclear weapons cannot shield its territory from the consequences of supporting cross-border terrorism. Deterrence is robust. The strategic community can exhale.

This reading is wrong. Not because the nuclear threshold was crossed — it wasn't. Not because the crisis wasn't managed — it was, after a fashion, through external pressure and back-channel negotiation that neither side is fully acknowledging. The triumphalist reading is wrong because it mistakes the outcome for the lesson, and because the lesson both sides have now drawn from a successfully managed crisis is the precise mechanism by which the next crisis will be harder to manage and more likely to occur.

The stability-instability paradox is one of the more counterintuitive propositions in nuclear scholarship. First elaborated in the context of NATO-Soviet competition and subsequently applied extensively to South Asia by scholars at the Belfer Center, the Stimson Center, and the Carnegie Endowment, the paradox holds that the very thing that makes large wars between nuclear-armed states unlikely — mutual assured destruction — simultaneously makes smaller conflicts more probable. Nuclear weapons provide both sides with a floor beneath which conventional fighting can occur without triggering nuclear exchange. They stabilise at the existential level. They destabilise at every level below it. States sheltered from catastrophic war by mutual deterrence have more room, not less, to prosecute limited conflict.

The paradox has been visible in South Asia since at least Kargil in 1999, when elements of the Pakistani army infiltrated positions across the Line of Control in Kashmir while both arsenals sat on alert. Pakistan calculated, correctly, that its nuclear deterrent would prevent India from widening the war to involve Pakistani heartland territory. India calculated, correctly, that Pakistan would not use nuclear weapons in response to the limited counteroffensive required to evict the infiltrators. Both calculations proved right. Nuclear deterrence worked at the strategic level; conventional warfare was prosecuted successfully below it. The paradox was demonstrated but not yet fully absorbed — neither side had reason to update its doctrine dramatically, because neither had tested what the floor would look like if India crossed the Line of Control rather than fighting on its own side of it.

Operation Sindoor moved that question from theoretical to empirical. The strikes on May 7, 2025, hit nine targets described by India as terrorist infrastructure, extended deeper into Pakistani territory than any Indian military operation since the 1971 war, and for the first time since that war included strikes on Pakistan's Punjab province — the political and military heartland, not the ambiguous contested territories of Kashmir. Pakistan responded with drone and missile strikes on Indian military targets; India struck again; nuclear warnings were issued; and then, after roughly four days of exchanges, a ceasefire took hold through a process that neither government has fully described but that almost certainly involved significant pressure from Washington, Riyadh, and Beijing.

The threshold was not crossed. But the knowledge gained on both sides is of a different order than anything Kargil produced.

The difference between Kargil and Sindoor is not primarily tactical, though the tactical stakes were higher. It is epistemic. After Kargil, both states understood the stability-instability paradox theoretically — they knew limited war under nuclear shadow was possible in principle. After Sindoor, they know it in practice. India has established, through action rather than assertion, that its tolerance for Pakistani nuclear signalling is limited, that it will respond to mass-casualty attacks with conventional strikes including on the heartland, and that it will absorb nuclear rhetoric without altering its military conduct. Pakistan has established that its nuclear deterrent remains credible at the existential level — India struck at infrastructure, not at military command, not at nuclear facilities, not at state survival — but that the deterrent cannot function as a blanket shield against conventional punishment for actions below that threshold.

Both conclusions are operationally rational. Both are also, in combination, the mechanism by which the next crisis will arrive with greater confidence, shorter deliberation time, and a reduced margin for the caution that kept Sindoor from becoming something worse.

India's new "deterrence by punishment" doctrine encodes this learning. The doctrine, now explicitly articulated by senior military officials, commits India to conventional retaliation for future mass-casualty attacks attributable to Pakistan-based actors. The intent is deterrent: make the cost of sponsoring cross-border terrorism unacceptably high. The operational logic is sound. The strategic dynamic it creates is not. A publicly declared retaliation doctrine establishes, for Pakistan's military planners, the precise threshold they need to calibrate future operations against. The doctrine intended to deter simultaneously maps the space within which the next provocation will be designed: small enough that India cannot credibly invoke the Punjab-strike threshold, or large enough that Pakistan's response to Indian retaliation will appear to domestic and international audiences as defensive necessity rather than escalatory choice.

Pakistan's post-Sindoor posture reinforces this concern. Having absorbed significant conventional strikes and emerged with the nuclear deterrent intact, territorial integrity formally preserved, and the status quo — including the unresolved Kashmir dispute and the continued presence of militant infrastructure — essentially unchanged, Pakistani planners have strong structural incentives to conclude that the May 2025 episode did not fundamentally alter the strategic utility of the proxy option. The ceasefire held. The relationship survived. The underlying conditions that produced Sindoor have not been addressed.

This structural logic sits in tension with a further complication: the sub-state actor problem. The Pahalgam attack of April 2025 — the killing of twenty-six civilians that triggered Sindoor — was carried out by actors operating in a grey zone of Pakistani state authority. Lashkar-e-Taiba and its affiliates have functioned for decades in a relationship with Pakistan's Inter-Services Intelligence that is neither complete state direction nor genuine non-state autonomy. The ISI has cultivated, funded, and at various moments attempted to rein in these groups, but the relationship is not a command hierarchy. It is a patronage network with considerable autonomous momentum.

The tension is real, and it needs stating directly: the essay's argument moves between two levels that do not map cleanly onto each other. At the state-actor level, Pakistani military planners may rationally calibrate future operations in response to India's declared doctrine. At the sub-state level, the actors most likely to produce the triggering event for the next crisis — an ISI faction, a militant commander, a network that has partly escaped state oversight — do not make their calculations in response to what a Pakistani general thinks India's retaliation threshold is. India's "deterrence by punishment" doctrine can influence Pakistani state decisions about support, resources, and restraint. It cannot straightforwardly deter actors that the Pakistani state itself cannot fully command. The Pahalgam attack may have been directed; it may have been facilitated; it may have been something that Pakistani intelligence knew about without being able to stop. The ceasefire negotiations and India's retaliation doctrine both assumed a level of Pakistani state control over the triggering event that the actual relationship between the Pakistani state and its proxy networks does not reliably support. The two logics — rational state calibration and autonomous proxy momentum — do not cancel each other. They coexist, which is precisely the problem.

There is a serious version of the counter-argument that deserves engagement rather than dismissal. Nuclear deterrence between India and Pakistan has, over twenty-five years of acknowledged nuclear status, prevented a large-scale conventional war of the kind that killed tens of thousands in 1971. Sindoor was managed — both sides showed constraint at decisive moments, neither government lost control of its military, and the ceasefire held without a subsequent exchange of strikes. The stability-instability paradox does not predict nuclear use; it predicts more frequent limited conflict. More frequent limited conflict that remains limited is, on one serious reading, preferable to infrequent catastrophic conflict. And the counter-factual — a non-nuclear South Asia — is not obviously safer, given the scale of conventional wars that preceded nuclear acquisition.

This case has genuine force. The issue is that it treats the management of Sindoor as a structural feature of the deterrence relationship rather than as a contingent outcome of favourable conditions. The preconditions for Sindoor's successful management — external actors willing and able to intervene, enough decision-making time on both sides for second thoughts, both governments with political space to accept a ceasefire without appearing to capitulate publicly — are not permanent features of the India-Pakistan relationship. They are variables. Each future crisis arrives with its own configuration of those variables, and confidence born of previous success is not a stabilising factor. It is a source of miscalculation.

The Cold War's record makes this point historically. The Cuban Missile Crisis is the canonical demonstration that nuclear deterrence works. The reconstructed historical record — assembled over decades of declassified documents, Politburo transcripts, and retrospective conference testimony — reveals how close the system came to failure at multiple points: Soviet submarine B-59, cut off from communications and under depth-charge attack, whose commander believed war had begun and nearly launched a nuclear torpedo; the U-2 that strayed into Soviet airspace on October 27 and prompted Soviet interceptors; the tactical nuclear weapons on the ground in Cuba, unknown to Kennedy's ExComm, whose use a field commander was authorised under certain conditions to order. The crisis was managed not by institutional robustness but by luck, individual restraint under extreme pressure, and improvisation at moments that planning had not anticipated.

South Asia has a fraction of the institutional infrastructure the superpowers eventually built across three decades of close calls. The hotlines, arms control frameworks, and established back-channel protocols that reduced superpower risk were constructed after crises demonstrated their necessity. South Asia is not building equivalent infrastructure at a rate commensurate with the frequency of crises it is experiencing, and the frequency, as the stability-instability paradox predicts, is rising rather than falling.

There is also a governance asymmetry problem that has received little analytical attention. India's elected government has clear constitutional authority over military decision-making and a civilian chain of command that is legible as a decision unit, whatever its political pressures. Pakistan's constitutional structure gives civilian governments formal authority that its military's political dominance has historically and extensively undermined. When an external broker needs to secure a ceasefire commitment under time pressure, the question of which Pakistani institution can actually deliver and sustain that commitment is not straightforward. The ceasefire of May 2025 held because it was in everyone's immediate interest and because the external pressure was intense enough and well-targeted enough to reach the relevant decision-makers on both sides. Whether that combination recurs in a future crisis under different conditions is not a question that the success of 2025 answers.

One further dynamic deserves attention before returning to the question of external pressure: the divergence between how each side has publicly narrated the Sindoor episode, and what that divergence does to the domestic political constraints on future decision-making. India's public narrative is, broadly, one of successful punishment — strikes conducted, targets destroyed, nuclear brinkmanship faced down, de-escalation accepted from a position of demonstrated strength. Pakistan's public narrative is, broadly, one of successful restraint — nuclear deterrence maintained, Indian aggression absorbed, ceasefire secured without capitulation. Both narratives serve domestic political purposes, and both are partly true. But they are structurally incompatible in ways that will constrain future decision-making on both sides. An Indian government that accepted a ceasefire after demonstrating conventional dominance has established a domestic political expectation of future willingness to do the same. A Pakistani government and military that presented the ceasefire as prudent restraint rather than concession have made it harder, not easier, to de-escalate quickly in a future crisis that touches the national honour more acutely. The asymmetric narratives of the same event are not merely a communications problem. They are a constraint on the decision space available to both governments when the next crisis arrives with its own domestic pressure and its own contested interpretation of what the prior episode proved.

The external pressure architecture is also less available now than it was a year ago. The Trump administration has disengaged from South Asian diplomacy. American military assets are committed to the Strait of Hormuz — where Iran's closure of the strait in March 2026 has absorbed the administration's strategic attention. Gulf states that helped broker the 2025 ceasefire are managing their own acute situation. The configuration that worked once was contingent on a specific geopolitical moment. It is not a structural feature of the region's crisis management capacity.

The language of "calling bluffs" misunderstands the nuclear relationship it purports to describe. A bluff is a threat without the intention or capacity to execute it. Pakistan's nuclear weapons are not a bluff. The Federation of American Scientists estimates Pakistan's arsenal at approximately 170 warheads — roughly double the Kargil-era figure. Pakistan has continued developing delivery systems, including low-yield tactical warheads explicitly designed for battlefield use, the Nasr short-range missile system, and a nascent sea-based deterrent capacity whose operational status remains uncertain but whose development is not. They remain real, deliverable, and sufficient to cause damage to Indian territory that no government in New Delhi would willingly accept. What India demonstrated in May 2025 was not that the bluff was hollow — it was that the deterrent sits at a specific and identifiable threshold: the survival of the Pakistani state, the physical integrity of its nuclear command and control, the credibility of its sovereign existence. Below that threshold, conventional punishment is possible. India located the floor. It did not eliminate it.

The triumphalist reading of Sindoor is relief dressed as strategic clarity. A significant conventional exchange occurred between two nuclear-armed states, and the world did not end. Treating the successful management of one crisis as evidence that the deterrence architecture is sound is a mistake — when what the episode demonstrated is that the architecture is contingent on external scaffolding that may not be present next time, that both sides have drawn lessons from Sindoor that will make the next confrontation more likely and its management harder, that India's new doctrine creates escalation dynamics it has not fully worked through, and that the sub-state actors most likely to produce the triggering event operate outside the deterrence calculus the doctrine assumes.

The stability-instability paradox has been made operational in South Asia. The bluff held. The belief it has produced in both capitals — that limited war under the nuclear shadow is manageable, that the other side's restraint can be counted on — is not a reassurance. It is the mechanism of the next crisis.
