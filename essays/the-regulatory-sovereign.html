The Regulatory Sovereign

May 2026

The Draghi report arrived in September 2024 like a diagnosis delivered to a patient who could not afford the treatment. Mario Draghi — the former ECB president who in 2012 declared he would do "whatever it takes" to save the euro, and then did — found that Europe was falling behind the United States and China across nearly every technology sector that would determine economic power in the following generation: advanced semiconductors, artificial intelligence, clean energy manufacturing, defence equipment. The productivity gap with the United States had widened for two consecutive decades. No European technology company built from scratch since the 1980s has crossed $100 billion in market value — a gap that ASML's $530 billion valuation makes more vivid rather than less, since ASML sells the machines that others use to build what Europe cannot. Its prescription required €800 billion in new annual investment — roughly 4.4 percent of EU GDP — sustained over a decade.

He offered no convincing answer to where the money would come from. Draghi proposed common EU debt issuance — a significant political ask — without specifying the revenue mechanism that would service it, and without securing the endorsement of the member states whose agreement it would require. The proposal was serious. The response was not.

The EU's political response was to commission further documents. Within eighteen months, the Commission had published a Competitiveness Compass (the strategic framework), a ReArm Europe plan (the defence component), an AI Action Plan (the technology component), a Clean Industrial Deal (the energy component), and — because no EU policy is complete without one — a simplification agenda to reduce the regulatory burden that the previous round of ambition had created. What the Commission had not published was a credible mechanism for raising €800 billion a year. It had not published this because no such mechanism exists within the European fiscal architecture.

This is the structural problem that the EU's "independence moment" — Ursula von der Leyen's phrase — cannot face directly. Europe's strategic demands and Europe's fiscal reality are not merely misaligned. They are architecturally incompatible. And the response to this incompatibility — more regulation, more standards-setting, more rules — is a form of displacement that mistakes the power to govern others' capacity for the possession of one's own. It is productive displacement, in the sense that European regulation genuinely shapes global behaviour. But it cannot resolve the underlying gap between what Europe needs to build and what it is currently capable of financing.

The convergence of demands Europe now faces is not a rhetorical device. It is a material fact — and calling it a trilemma, though the term is inexact (the three demands do not logically preclude each other), captures something accurate: they are financially incompatible when faced simultaneously on the current fiscal architecture.

The first demand is military. At the Hague Summit in 2025, NATO allies committed to defence spending of 5 percent of GDP by 2035, with at least 3.5 percent meeting the traditional NATO definition of defence expenditure. For context: Europe's thirty NATO members spent a combined $864 billion on defence in 2025, a 14 percent increase that made headlines but brought only twenty-two of them to the 2 percent threshold that had been the standing target for a decade. Getting from the current European average of approximately 2.5 percent of GDP to 5 percent means more than doubling defence expenditure. For France, Germany, and the United Kingdom combined, that represents an additional $400–500 billion annually.

The second demand is energy. Europe's dependence on Russian gas was not a policy failure so much as a policy design — cheap, reliable energy imported from a stable supplier, allowing European industry to remain competitive without decarbonising. That design collapsed in 2022. European industrial electricity prices now run three to five times US levels, a structural competitiveness disadvantage that has accelerated deindustrialisation in energy-intensive sectors and pushed production decisions toward the United States and Asia. Reversing this requires massive investment in renewable generation capacity, long-distance transmission infrastructure, energy storage, and — for the most energy-intensive industries — industrial hydrogen. The Commission's Clean Industrial Deal acknowledges that full decarbonisation at competitive prices requires capital deployment of €200 billion annually above existing trajectories.

The third demand is industrial. The Draghi diagnosis is, at its core, about compound underinvestment in the technologies that determine long-run productivity. Europe holds approximately 10 percent of global AI compute capacity, the great majority of it rented from American hyperscalers rather than domestically owned. It has no frontier AI laboratory producing models competitive with OpenAI, Anthropic, or Google DeepMind. ASML — the Dutch company that manufactures the extreme-ultraviolet lithography machines without which advanced semiconductors cannot be made — is Europe's single genuinely irreplaceable technology firm, and its contribution is to sell machines to others rather than to build European chip production at scale. The EU has one fabrication facility capable of sub-7nm production: TSMC's Dresden plant, a foreign company's European outpost.

These three demands share a property: they are material rather than normative. They require capital investment in physical infrastructure, production capacity, and military hardware. Regulation cannot substitute for them. The key question is why Europe's response has nonetheless been primarily regulatory.

The answer is structural. The euro area's fiscal framework was designed for a different strategic environment. The Stability and Growth Pact — its 3 percent deficit limit, its 60 percent debt-to-GDP target — was a mechanism for preventing fiscal free-riding in a monetary union without political union. Member states sharing a currency but maintaining separate fiscal authorities needed rules to prevent any one government from externalising its borrowing costs onto others. The framework worked, in a limited sense, for that purpose.

It was not designed for an era in which the United States has conditionalised its security guarantee, Chinese industrial policy is producing genuine alternatives in semiconductors and electric vehicles, and Russian aggression has exposed the cost of energy dependence. The fiscal architecture of the eurozone assumes that the major strategic threats facing European members are economic (inflation, debt crisis, current account imbalances), manageable through market discipline and fiscal rules. It has no mechanism for the collective investment required when the threats are geopolitical.

The debt levels of Europe's major economies compound the problem. France runs a deficit of 6 percent of GDP with a debt-to-GDP ratio of 113 percent; Italy's ratio stands at 135 percent; Greece's at 151 percent. Germany has recently amended its constitutional debt brake to allow defence spending above 1 percent of GDP to be excluded — a historically significant liberalisation — but Germany's willingness to borrow for its own defence does not produce the joint EU debt instrument that would allow collective investment at the required scale.

Next Generation EU was the exception that proves the rule. The €750 billion recovery fund, agreed in 2020, represented genuine fiscal innovation: joint EU debt issuance, a precedent that had been resisted for decades. Its deployment since 2021 demonstrated that joint issuance was legally and politically feasible. But NGEU was a one-time response to an acute crisis, not a structural funding mechanism. Its successor — the ReArm Europe plan's €150 billion SAFE facility — is a lending instrument rather than a grant mechanism, and its scale is an order of magnitude below what the strategic demands require.

The result is what might be called a fiscal trilemma within the trilemma: Germany has the fiscal space and the constitutional permission to spend on defence, but its surplus-oriented model restrains it; France and Italy have the strategic ambition but not the fiscal capacity; and the EU level lacks the taxing and spending authority to bridge the gap. European rearmament is €800 billion in ambition sitting on a €150 billion mechanism.

Against this backdrop, European regulation looks less like a strategic choice and more like an institutional reflex. The EU does what it is institutionally capable of doing: it makes rules.

The AI Act, entering full application across 2025 and 2026, is the world's first comprehensive legal framework for artificial intelligence. Its risk-based architecture — prohibiting certain applications outright, imposing extensive obligations on high-risk systems — is genuinely consequential. No major AI company deploying models in Europe can disregard it. OpenAI restructured its European operations partly in response to its provisions. The compliance burden it imposes on American, Chinese, and European AI developers alike is real, and real in Europe's favour in the sense that European regulators rather than American or Chinese ones set the frame.

But the AI Act does not build European AI capacity. It regulates systems developed primarily in California and deployed in Frankfurt, Paris, and Warsaw. It shapes the behaviour of American infrastructure without reducing European dependence on it. The EU has no frontier model. It has no hyperscale cloud provider. The act is a regulatory achievement mounted atop a strategic dependency, like a sophisticated planning code for a city that cannot afford to build.

The Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism — in force since 2026 — imposes tariffs on carbon-intensive imports from countries without comparable carbon pricing. It prevents carbon leakage: European industry that has paid the cost of decarbonisation cannot simply be undercut by cheaper imports from jurisdictions that have not. It is a defensible instrument. It does not fund decarbonisation. The EU Emissions Trading System generates approximately €40 billion annually, recycled through the Innovation Fund for green technology. This is real money and it matters at the margin. It is not €200 billion annually.

The Digital Markets Act and the Digital Services Act impose gatekeeping and content moderation obligations on the largest digital platforms. All the designated gatekeepers are American: Alphabet, Apple, Meta, Amazon, Microsoft, TikTok (Chinese-owned). Europe has regulated foreign digital infrastructure without producing domestic alternatives. The largest European technology companies by market capitalisation — ASML, SAP, Spotify — are not competitive with the companies being regulated in the domains the regulations address.

This pattern has a name. The "Brussels Effect" — the mechanism by which EU regulation becomes global standard because global companies cannot maintain separate product lines for European and non-European markets — is well-documented and real. GDPR reshaped global data privacy practices. The AI Act will constrain frontier AI deployment worldwide in ways that no EU-domestic AI developer would have produced. European regulatory power has genuine reach.

What the Brussels Effect cannot do is resolve strategic dependencies. You cannot regulate your way to semiconductor fabrication capacity, frontier AI models, indigenous cloud infrastructure, or a credible defence industrial base. The power to set rules for others' technology is real; it is not the same as having the technology. Standards-setting works on the normative plane — it shapes behaviour within existing market structures. It cannot substitute for investment in the infrastructure that those markets depend on.

The best defence of European regulatory strategy as genuinely strategic — rather than merely the best available option — makes a different claim. The EU's market is large enough, and exclusion from it costly enough, that regulatory power constitutes genuine geopolitical leverage. The United States complies with GDPR not from conviction but from calculation. The same calculation will apply to CBAM, to the AI Act, to future European digital regulation. On this view, market-access power is European power, appropriate to a polity that lacks military sovereignty and can build on its regulatory infrastructure in ways that military investment cannot easily replicate.

This is not wrong. It is incomplete in ways that matter. Market-access leverage operates within the domain of commercial behaviour — product design, data handling, market structure. It does not operate within the domain of physical infrastructure, military capability, or energy security. When Iranian Shahed drones struck AWS data centres in the UAE and Bahrain in March 2026 — forcing cloud services offline across the region and disrupting banking, payments, and enterprise software — the incident marked the first deliberate military attack on commercial hyperscale infrastructure. It fell entirely outside the domain in which European regulatory power has any purchase. The AI Act imposes nothing on Iranian drone programmes. CBAM does nothing for European energy independence when gas supplies are weaponised. DMA compliance by American platforms does not reduce Europe's structural dependence on American compute.

The strategic competition now underway between the United States and China — over compute infrastructure, semiconductor production, energy systems, and military capability — is being contested at a level where European regulatory power provides no leverage. This is not an incidental limitation. It reflects the more fundamental point that the three strategic demands Europe faces — rearmament, energy independence, industrial competitiveness — are demands for material capacity, and material capacity requires capital that European institutions are not currently structured to raise at the required scale.

The phrase "independence moment" captures something real about the current conjuncture: the narrowness of the window, the irreversibility of the choices, the gap between what the moment requires and what the institutions can deliver. Von der Leyen's formulation, stripped of its optimism, is a reasonably accurate description of Europe's predicament.

What it obscures is the nature of the choice. The EU is not facing a gap that can be closed by better regulation, clearer strategic frameworks, or more ambitious Commission communications. It is facing a structural incompatibility between its strategic demands and its fiscal architecture — one that can be resolved in two ways, and only two.

The first is genuine fiscal integration: common debt issuance at the scale required, common revenue mechanisms (a European-level tax, whether on carbon, digital activity, or financial transactions), and the political transfer of sovereignty over spending decisions to an institution capable of making them. This is what NGEU proved feasible in principle. It is what resistance from northern member states, constitutional constraints in Germany and others, and the democratic legitimacy problems of European-level taxation have prevented in practice.

The second is managed strategic dependence: a deliberate, eyes-open acknowledgment that Europe will remain dependent on American security guarantees, American compute infrastructure, and American capital markets for the foreseeable future, and that European strategy should be designed accordingly. This would mean something more than the current posture: formal burden-sharing agreements within NATO that clarify what the US guarantee actually covers; structured access arrangements with American hyperscalers that treat cloud infrastructure as dual-use strategic capacity rather than commercial service; and a European diplomatic strategy explicitly premised on maintaining the transatlantic relationship rather than one that uses "independence" as a cover for ambiguity. Managed dependence is not the same as subordination. It is the acknowledgment that interdependence, consciously structured, is more durable than declared autonomy that is not backed by capacity.

Both paths are available. Both are difficult. The current path — ambitious strategic documents on a fiscal architecture that cannot support them, regulatory ambition substituted for material investment, independence declared rather than built — is neither.

What the Draghi report got right was the diagnosis. What it missed, or could not say directly, is that the treatment requires not more European ambition but different European institutions. Rules cannot build what infrastructure requires capital to build. The regulatory sovereign can govern a market it does not control. It cannot, by governing alone, become sovereign over the forces that will determine which societies thrive in the decade ahead.
