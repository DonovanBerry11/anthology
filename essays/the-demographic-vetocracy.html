The Demographic Vetocracy

May 2026 · Published 7 May 2026, 1:31 AM EST

When Emmanuel Macron's government finally pushed France's pension reform into law in April 2023 — raising the retirement age from sixty-two to sixty-four after months of strikes, garbage piling in the streets of Paris, and the invocation of Article 49.3 to bypass a parliamentary vote that the government would have lost — the reform was widely interpreted as a tortured proof of state capacity. Tortured because the executive had to walk over the legislature's prerogatives, weather a no-confidence motion, and absorb a collapse in approval ratings; proof because in the end a politically suicidal but actuarially necessary measure had been carried.

Two and a half years later, on 12 November 2025, the National Assembly voted 255 to 146 to suspend it. The suspension was the price Prime Minister Sébastien Lecornu paid the Socialists to avoid a no-confidence motion that would have brought down his government, smuggled into a ratifying amendment of the 2026 social-security budget. It freezes the retirement age at 62 years and nine months until after the 2027 presidential election.

What is striking about the sequence is not the political fragility — pension reforms have always been fragile — but the demonstration that, in a contemporary advanced democracy with declining demographic ratios, a reform whose burden falls on older citizens cannot be made durable even when constitutional emergency procedures have been deployed to carry it. The political system did not produce the policy; when forced to, it did not retain it. What survived the strikes was overturned by a vote.

This essay argues that the convergent failure of advanced democracies to reform housing supply, pension finance, fiscal sustainability, and the climate transition is not a series of independent policy debates that have, by coincidence, all become hard to resolve at the same time. They are a single structural phenomenon. In each case the reform's costs would fall predominantly on older citizens, while its benefits would accrue to younger or future ones. In each case the political system has produced not failure but a recognisable equilibrium — the equilibrium of an electorate whose median voter is now old enough to make intergenerational redistribution a losing proposition at the ballot box. The phenomenon is large enough, recurrent enough and structured enough to deserve its own name. Call it the demographic vetocracy.

In 1950, around 8 per cent of the population of Europe and North America was aged sixty-five or above. By 2024 that share had risen to close to 20 per cent in the European Union and approaching 18 per cent in the United States. The EU old-age dependency ratio is projected to reach roughly 53 per cent by 2060 — fewer than two working-age adults per retiree, against the four-to-one ratio of a generation ago — and on Eurostat's central projection the EU's working-age population will shrink by 13 per cent over the same window. In the United States, the Census Bureau's 2024 voter survey reports turnout at 74.7 per cent for citizens aged sixty-five and above, against 47.7 per cent for those aged eighteen to twenty-four. Older voters do not merely outnumber the young more than they did half a century ago; they participate at substantially higher rates.

The political arithmetic is not subtle. In the average advanced democracy the median voter is now in her mid-fifties or older — a homeowner, often with significant equity, approaching or in retirement, with more wealth than at any earlier point in her life and the prospect of inheriting the principal residence of her parents. She will receive state-funded healthcare for a longer period than any cohort in history. The reforms that fiscal, demographic and climate trajectories require would impose discrete and identifiable costs on her, while delivering benefits to her children, her grandchildren or to people not yet born.

This is not, in the first instance, a moral argument. The median voter is responding rationally to the bargain being offered: she is being asked to vote against her direct, near-term economic interest in favour of a long-run interest she will not personally realise. Democratic theory has rarely produced examples of majorities voting against their own immediate distributive position, and the cases that exist usually involve proximate wartime threats or shared catastrophe. What is being asked of the older voter is a sustained, voluntary, economically meaningful sacrifice to repair a state of affairs she did not single-handedly cause and from whose repair she draws no significant benefit. That she declines is not pathology. It is the predictable behaviour of a rational agent in an electoral system that gives her the deciding vote.

Pension finance is the cleanest example because it is the most arithmetically transparent. France's 2023 reform aimed to address a deficit projected at roughly €13.5 billion annually by 2030; the 2025 suspension restored that trajectory at an additional cost of €1.9 billion in 2027 alone. The pattern is not French. Across Italy, Spain, the Netherlands and Greece, every meaningful tightening of pension entitlements over the past decade has been reversed at the next election, hollowed out through grandfathering, or accompanied by cash transfers greater than the savings extracted. In Germany, the formal retirement age phases up to sixty-seven by 2031, but a carve-out for workers with forty-five contribution years has kept effective retirement closer to sixty-four. Sweden's notional defined-contribution architecture, often held up as the country that did pension reform properly, was the product of a five-party working group operating under acute fiscal stress in the early 1990s. Italy and Poland adopted similar architectures in 1995 and 1999 but neither has run them without continuous renegotiation of the carve-outs that protect older workers. The Swedish reform is harder to replicate than to admire.

Housing produces the same logic in a different register. The crisis has been treated almost universally as a problem of supply and zoning: there are not enough homes, the rules make it too hard to build, the pro-supply movement is the necessary corrective. The framing leaves an awkward question unanswered. If the answer is as obvious as zoning reform, why do the reforms not pass? Because the constituency that turns out at local council elections — homeowners over fifty — is the constituency whose principal asset would lose value if they did. A serious supply intervention is, in effect, a transfer from incumbent owners to renters and aspiring buyers, and the incumbent owner is not merely opposed: she is organised, attentive and electorally decisive at exactly the level of government — municipal, state, regional — where housing policy is set. Supply does expand somewhat, in places, on terms that protect existing owners. What it does not do is expand enough to reverse price trajectories, because the kind of expansion that would do so is the kind a system tilted towards older homeowners cannot produce.

In the United Kingdom, more than half of first-time buyers now depend on parental contributions for their deposit; in the United States, the spread between mean and median house prices has widened markedly. Housing is becoming, by degrees, the marker of inherited advantage rather than earned saving. Reform — supply expansion, taxation of imputed rents, removal of mortgage-interest deductibility, capital-gains treatment of principal residences — is, individually, an electoral suicide note. The agenda is shaped before reforms reach a vote: parties promise to "fix housing" in language general enough to commit to nothing in particular, and deliver, when in office, programmes to support first-time buyers — programmes that increase demand without expanding supply, propping up prices to the benefit of the incumbent.

The fiscal case is more familiar but no less structural. The Congressional Budget Office's February 2026 baseline projects US debt held by the public rising from 101 per cent of GDP today to 120 per cent by 2036; interest payments now exceed defence spending and continue to grow. Stabilisation requires, in any honest accounting, action on Social Security, Medicare and the tax base; none has been politically available to either party. Each cycle produces marginal adjustments that do not bend the trajectory. The CBO's "alternative scenario" — the one that incorporates the political reality that current-law triggers will not be allowed to bite when they arrive — is now the de facto baseline. The debate is no longer whether the numbers will be met but how the failure to meet them will be financed.

Climate is the hardest case to discuss without imputing motives, and the demographic mechanism is one factor among several rather than the dominant one. The transition from fossil fuels to a low-carbon economy requires investment now whose benefits arrive in decades; the bulk of those benefits accrues to people who do not vote — children, future cohorts, populations in the global South. The coalitions that have most effectively resisted carbon pricing, fuel-tax increases and accelerated fossil-fuel retirement skew older, even where the immediate trigger is industrial or regional. The gilets jaunes had a median age in the mid-forties and a substantial older tail; the cohort least mobilised was the under-thirties. None of this denies the legitimate grievances of working-class older voters whose energy bills are an outsize share of disposable income. It notes only that the political economy of the climate transition runs into the same constraint as housing and pensions: those who vote do not benefit from the policy proposed, and they vote accordingly.

What unifies these cases is that each of the standard explanations applied to them sits downstream of the same demographic mechanism. Interest-group capture is real, but the interest groups whose veto matters most are demographically defined: AARP in the United States, retired-worker unions in France, local homeowner associations everywhere. Polarisation is real, but cross-cutting polarisation cannot explain why both centre-left and centre-right governments in France, Italy, Germany, Britain and the United States have failed to deliver the same reforms. Institutional veto points matter, but the most consistent veto point is not the upper chamber or the constitutional court; it is the next election, and the next election turns on a median voter whose age has shifted decisively over forty years. The demographic vetocracy is the common factor across the four cases, even where it is not the dominant one in each.

It is worth noting what this argument is not. It is not the claim that the elderly vote as a bloc on intergenerational issues; they do not, and older voters' policy preferences vary by party, region and economic position. The claim is more specific. When reform proposals impose concentrated costs on the older cohort and disperse benefits across younger or future cohorts, the political system tends to internalise the constraint before the reforms reach a vote. This is a claim about agenda-setting and policy survival, not about voting behaviour at the moment of decision. The mechanism is not that the elderly always vote no; it is that politicians with electoral incentives observe how the elderly will vote and shape proposals accordingly.

The British general election of 2017 is the cleanest documented case. The Conservative manifesto proposed an asset-tested social-care charge — quickly nicknamed the "dementia tax" — under which the elderly would draw down their housing equity to finance their own care. The proposal was, as fiscal policy, defensible. The political response from older voters was severe enough that the policy was withdrawn within four days, and the Conservatives lost their parliamentary majority in an election they had been expected to win comfortably. No major British party has revived asset-tested social-care funding in the eight years since. The withdrawal is the policy. The same dynamic is visible in the absence of proposals as much as in their fates: no Republican or Democratic platform in the United States proposes a Social Security cut or a Medicare premium rise, even where individual legislators acknowledge in private the inevitability of one or the other.

Nor is the dynamic unprecedented. Senior voters have always had higher turnout, and intergenerational redistribution has always been politically harder than intragenerational. What has changed is the magnitude. When 8 per cent of the electorate was over sixty-five, the median voter sat well below the age at which these policies became electorally costly. When 20 per cent is over sixty-five, and turnout multiplies that share at the ballot box, the median voter sits past the threshold. The change is quantitative; the threshold is real; the political consequences are not.

Four counter-arguments deserve to be taken seriously. The first is that the argument proves too much: democracies have, over time, undertaken difficult intergenerational reforms. But the cases that exist tend to share specific conditions — fiscal crisis severe enough to make no-reform more painful than reform, cross-party coalitions willing to share the political cost, and the absence of a populist outside option to which displaced voters could defect. None of those conditions reliably obtains today. Crisis is delayed by accommodative monetary policy and dollar reserve status; coalitions are more fragile; the populist outside option is ubiquitous. The cohort that would absorb pension or healthcare cuts now has somewhere to go.

The second is that technological change might dissolve the trade-off: AI-driven productivity gains might, in principle, fund pensions and the climate transition without requiring sacrifice from any cohort. This is the Pollyanna case. It rests on assumptions about distribution that the literature does not warrant. If the gains accrue to capital, they do not flow into the wage base on which pension finance depends; if they accrue to a narrow cohort of workers, they do not generalise to the broader fiscal base. Whether technology can substitute for the vetocracy is the same question as who will own the AI productivity revolution, and the honest answer at present is: not on current trajectories.

The third is that the demographic structure will, in time, correct itself: the boomer cohort will pass and the median voter will shift back. This is true in the very long run and almost irrelevant to the policy questions on the table. Pension finance, climate transition and housing reform require action in the next ten to twenty years, throughout which the older cohort will continue to vote at high rates. By the time demographic correction occurs, the losses compounded by inaction will themselves have foreclosed many of the reforms now contemplated.

The fourth is that the mechanism on offer is wealth, not age — that a young homeowner with significant equity behaves like an old one, and the demographic frame is the surface of a deeper story about asset position. The objection has force. An analytically purer name would be the asset-holder vetocracy. But in advanced democracies over the past three decades, age and asset position have come to coincide closely enough that the distinction does little work. US over-fifty homeownership runs at around seventy-five per cent; the under-thirty-five rate sits below forty per cent and falling. Mortgage equity, defined-benefit pension claims and inherited residential property all map onto the same age distribution; the retired draw state-funded healthcare regardless of asset position. If the alignment between age and asset were to weaken — through inheritance flows, redistributive taxation or a housing-market collapse — the mechanism would weaken with it. None of these is currently in train.

The deeper question this raises is not policy but constitutional. The classical defence of democratic decision-making rests on the proposition that majorities have the right to allocate burdens within the polity. That defence becomes harder to sustain when the majority is also the cohort whose burdens are being allocated, and when those burdens are passed forward in time to people who are not yet voters or not yet alive. Democracies have institutional mechanisms designed to protect intertemporal interests against majority decision-making — constitutional courts, fiscal rules, central bank independence, environmental impact assessments. What is striking about the contemporary moment is how systematically each of these is being eroded at exactly the point in the demographic cycle when they would most need to bind.

The fiscal rules of the eurozone are being relaxed; the United States has no functioning fiscal rule at all. Constitutional courts have come under sustained political attack, with appointments politicised and decisions challenged. Central bank independence is being contested more openly than at any point since the 1970s. The institutional hedges against intergenerational extraction are coming down precisely as the demographic incentive for that extraction is peaking. It would be too clean to call this no coincidence: the populist movements weakening these institutions draw support from a wider coalition than the older voter alone, and their motives are not reducible to a single mechanism. But the older voter is reliably present in the coalition, and the institutions being weakened are precisely those that would constrain her position. The vetocracy operates on the rules of the game as well as on the moves within them.

What this implies is harder to face than the policy specifics. If the institutional architecture that constrains intergenerational extraction is eroding at the same time that the demographic incentive for that extraction is peaking, the result is not policy stalemate but a steady transfer of resources from younger and future cohorts to older ones — through unfunded entitlements, through underbuilt housing, through delayed climate action, through accumulated public debt held disproportionately by funds that finance the older cohort's consumption. The political system is not idle. It is doing exactly what its incentives say to do, and it is doing it with increasing efficiency.

The question this leaves is what the cohort being extracted from will do when the structure of the bargain becomes too obvious to deny. Political stability in advanced democracies has rested on an implicit intergenerational contract: each generation accepts costs imposed by the older one in expectation that it will, in turn, impose them on the next. When the contract becomes too transparently asymmetric — when the next generation can no longer expect to be made whole by the one that follows it, because the one that follows it will not exist in the numbers required — the contract begins to dissolve. Younger voters in advanced democracies have, across most surveys recorded over the past five years, registered as more economically pessimistic and more attracted to anti-system platforms than their parents were at equivalent ages. The interpretation is contested. What is harder to contest is that the economic bargain offered to those parents is not the one their children are now being offered. Whether the response takes the form of exit — emigration, civic withdrawal, the lower fertility that compounds the demographic asymmetry — or voice — political mobilisation against the architecture itself — the durability of the current arrangement is not what its beneficiaries appear to assume. A vetocracy can hold for a long time. It cannot hold forever.
