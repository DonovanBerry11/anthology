Hostage Infrastructure

May 2026

Gulf sovereign AI capital is doing what petrodollars did: purchasing the American security guarantee. Saudi Arabia's $40 billion Humain fund, the UAE's $50 billion AI infrastructure commitment, Qatar's comparable mobilisation — these are not technology bets but security trades, replicating the logic by which Gulf oil revenues were routed into US Treasuries and defence contracts after 1973 and cemented the relationship for half a century. Resource wealth converts into US financial claims; the claims convert into protection. The mechanism is identical. What is different is the nature of the asset being offered, and that difference undermines the logic the arrangement depends on.

Petrodollars worked as a security instrument because they were liquid. A Treasury held in Riyadh can be sold, moved, converted, or pledged independently of what is happening on the ground. Its value does not depend on whether American carrier groups actually show up; the financial claim precedes the security event and survives its absence. The asset the Gulf is now deploying as its claim on US protection is a hyperscale data centre: fixed, physical, and located within the geography that defines the threat. CSIS identified the implication after Iran's drone campaign demonstrated in 2026 that Gulf territory is reachable at scale with cheap munitions — the infrastructure the Gulf is using to purchase US protection may be the first asset destroyed if that protection is called on and proves insufficient.

This produces an asymmetry petrodollar recycling never generated. The financial claim functions as a security instrument because its value is independent of the security environment. The compute claim is not — its value is conditional on the security environment holding. The Gulf is not offering a financial stake in the United States; it is offering hostage infrastructure: assets whose operational continuity requires the guarantee they are supposed to purchase. That inversion — where the collateral depends on the guarantee rather than the guarantee depending on the collateral — is the core formal difference between the two arrangements. In the petrodollar model, a deteriorating security environment increases the value of the financial relationship. In the compute model, a deteriorating security environment destroys the collateral.

The individual incentives are not irrational. US firms receive capital at the scale required to build infrastructure the domestic economy cannot fund alone. Gulf states secure political entrenchment in US strategic priorities at a moment when Washington's foreign commitments have become openly transactional. Both sides get something real. But the bargain differs from petrodollar recycling in one way that matters for its durability: petrodollars created a self-reinforcing relationship precisely because the financial asset retained its value during crises and therefore continued to motivate the guarantee. Compute capital creates the reverse: if the guarantee is tested and fails, the assets that purchased it are the first casualties.
