The Ratchet

May 2026 · Published 8 May 2026, 1:52 PM EST

There is a mechanism operating in the US fiscal position that is less discussed than the deficit and more consequential than the debt ceiling: debt rollover at permanently higher rates. Each year, roughly a third of outstanding Treasury debt matures and must be refinanced. The bonds issued at the historic lows of 2020 — when the ten-year yield fell to 0.52 percent, the lowest on record for US Treasuries — and at the lower rates that persisted into early 2021 are now rolling over between 4 and 4.5 percent. Each refunding at the higher rate adds permanently to the baseline of annual interest expenditure. Unlike a spending increase, it cannot be reversed by legislation. Unlike a deficit, it does not require new decisions to persist. It simply accumulates.

Approximately $9 trillion of Treasury debt matures in 2026. Rolled at rates two to three percentage points above original issuance, this cohort alone locks in an additional $180 to $270 billion in annual interest costs — before any new borrowing, before any new spending, before any policy change by any future administration. The Congressional Budget Office projects net interest costs matching and then overtaking defence in annual federal outlays — a crossing that current trajectories place within this decade. The interest line in the federal budget is not a policy choice awaiting reversal. It is an arithmetic consequence of decisions already made.

There are three theoretical exits from the ratchet, and none is clean. Rate collapse — a sustained return to near-zero yields — is not available in an environment of persistent inflation and a Federal Reserve that has announced no such intention. Inflation erodes the real value of fixed-rate debt over time, which is how the postwar debt load — over 100 percent of GDP in 1946 — was reduced to manageable levels by the 1970s, but this mechanism works only on fixed-rate debt already issued; it does nothing to prevent new debt from being issued at the inflation-adjusted higher nominal rates that the inflation itself requires. Reducing the real debt burden by tolerating sustained inflation is not a costless resolution — it is a different kind of fiscal deterioration, redistributing the burden rather than eliminating it. Repudiation is not a policy option for a reserve currency sovereign, and using it would eliminate the reserve currency status that makes the accumulation tolerable in the first place.

What distinguishes this from a conventional fiscal crisis is that the rollover mechanism does not require a yield spike to operate — though the structural deterioration it produces may, in time, contribute to one. A yield spike forces a reckoning. A debt ceiling breach produces a constitutional confrontation. The rollover mechanism requires neither — it operates through refinancings that are individually routine, reported in Treasury press releases, absorbed by markets without incident, and collectively eliminating the fiscal space available to any government that inherits the position.
