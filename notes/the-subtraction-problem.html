The Subtraction Problem

2026-05-07 · Published 7 May 2026, 1:12 PM EST

US net migration turned negative in 2025 for the first time in at least half a century. This is being covered as an immigration enforcement story — a political achievement or a humanitarian crisis, depending on the outlet. It is both of those things and also a structural labour economics story that the dominant political coverage has largely bypassed.

The relevant fact is this: since 2008, virtually all net growth in the US labour force has come from immigration. The US fertility rate has been below the 2.1 replacement threshold since at least that year, which means native-born cohorts entering the workforce are smaller than those aging out of it. The domestic working-age population is not growing on its own terms. For fifteen years, the economic model underpinning nominal GDP expansion, the consumer spending baseline, and the Social Security contribution schedule has rested on a single variable: net international migration adding approximately one million workers annually to the labour supply.

That variable has now been reversed by deliberate policy. The workers being deported or deterred are disproportionately working-age — prime contributors to the payroll tax base. The fiscal savings from enforcement are real: reduced public service costs, lower transfer payments. The fiscal costs are structural: a deteriorating dependency ratio, a narrowing payroll tax base, a compounding drag on nominal GDP that does not appear in any single quarter's figures but accumulates without interruption. Penn Wharton Budget Model's 2025 analysis estimated that net fiscal losses from mass deportation significantly exceed enforcement savings over a ten-year horizon. The Congressional Budget Office has not published a comparable mass-deportation scenario analysis — that absence is itself informative about where political attention is directed.

The policy instruments being offered as substitutes cannot close this gap. Tariffs do not add workers to the labour force. Deregulation does not. Tax cuts do not. AI productivity growth may partially offset declining labour input over time, but it operates on a different timescale from the contraction now occurring, and productivity gains do not substitute for the workers needed to staff logistics networks, care facilities, construction sites, and agricultural operations in the near term. The fertility rate of 1.6 is not a correctable policy error — it is a demographic fact with a fifteen-to-twenty-year lag before any response reaches the labour market. There is no domestic pipeline to replace what is being removed.

The political economy of the error is not mysterious. The benefits of enforcement are visible, immediate, and attributable. The costs materialise slowly, distribute diffusely, and are assigned to other causes — global slowdown, technological displacement, sector-specific adjustment. This is the structural incentive to continue despite the arithmetic, and why the labour story is not being told at the volume the numbers demand. It is not a forecast. It is a subtraction problem, and the subtraction is already underway.
