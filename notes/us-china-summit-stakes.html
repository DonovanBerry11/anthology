The Beijing Tea Ceremony: What the Trump-Xi Summit Actually Settled — Further Reading

May 2026 · Published 15 May 2026, 12:14 AM EST

The language of the joint communiqué matters more than it might appear. When the United States and China agreed to describe their relationship as 'constructive, strategic and stable,' they were consciously borrowing from a diplomatic vocabulary that both sides have used before — and abandoned before. The Obama-era framing of a 'new type of great power relationship' followed a similar logic: establish a conceptual container for the rivalry, then fill it with managed disagreements. That framework collapsed under the weight of the South China Sea disputes and the early trade tensions of the mid-2010s. The question worth sitting with is whether the new formulation is more durable, or simply more urgent because both sides now have reasons — the Iran conflict, AI competition, currency instability — to prefer a stable holding pattern over open confrontation. Xi's warning about Taiwan being capable of producing a 'highly dangerous situation' was not new language; it is language Beijing has used consistently since the Third Taiwan Strait Crisis in 1996. What is new is the context in which it is being delivered. Trump is managing an active war in the Middle East, markets that are only just recovering from a turbulent spring, and a domestic political coalition that contains both hawkish voices on China and business interests desperate to restore supply chains. Xi, for his part, is presiding over an economy that has not fully recovered its pre-pandemic growth trajectory and a property sector that remains structurally impaired. Neither leader arrived in Beijing with the luxury of maximalism. The deeper structural issue is that summits of this kind produce what scholars of international relations call 'negative peace' — the absence of immediate escalation rather than the presence of genuine agreement. The ten Chinese firms cleared to receive Nvidia chips represent a transactional concession, not a reconfiguration of the underlying technology competition. Both governments have spent the last four years building parallel semiconductor ecosystems precisely because each anticipates that the other cannot be relied upon as a long-term supplier. A single licensing decision does not reverse that logic. It is also worth understanding the domestic audience dynamics at work. Xi cannot be seen to concede on Taiwan without serious political cost inside the Chinese system, where the Taiwan question has been elevated to a matter of national legitimacy over decades of official rhetoric. Trump cannot be seen to soften on China without alienating a significant portion of his base. The result is that both leaders have an incentive to stage cooperation on peripheral issues — chip licenses, climate working groups, fentanyl enforcement coordination — while leaving the structural fault lines untouched. This is not cynicism; it is the ordinary grammar of great-power diplomacy. The more useful frame for evaluating what Beijing actually settled is to ask not what was agreed, but what was not said. There was no joint statement on Taiwan's political status. There was no framework for managing incidents in the South China Sea or the Taiwan Strait. There was no agreed protocol for AI systems with military applications. The silence on those questions is the real communiqué.
