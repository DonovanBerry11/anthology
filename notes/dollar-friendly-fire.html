Dollar Friendly Fire

May 2026 · Published 9 May 2026, 3:46 AM EST

Dollar sanctions work once. Each subsequent use subsidises the alternatives.

The United States controls access to the dollar clearing system. Any bank, firm, or government conducting dollar-denominated transactions routes through correspondent accounts held at US banks, which are subject to US jurisdiction. Exclusion from this network — through secondary sanctions, asset freezes, or SWIFT disconnection — is the coercive instrument. It has worked. Iran lost roughly 30 percent of its GDP between 2012 and 2015 as sanctions cut off oil revenues and collapsed import financing. The weapon's potency on first application is not in question.

The coercive instrument and the erosion instrument are the same tool. Every state that observes sanctions applied learns an identical fact: dollar reserve accumulation creates a specific vulnerability. Reserves held in US Treasuries can be frozen. Correspondent relationships can be severed. The entire chain of dollar-denominated trade can be cut at the US end. The rational response is to reduce exposure — not immediately, not publicly, and not completely, since the network is still too large to exit cleanly, but incrementally, at the margin, through parallel infrastructure that does not route through New York.

The construction of that infrastructure is measurable. China's Cross-Border Interbank Payment System processed $12 trillion in transactions in 2023, up from near-zero in 2015. Russia and China settled 90 percent of their bilateral trade in rubles and yuan in 2023, against roughly 30 percent in 2021, the year before Russia's reserves were frozen. The mBridge central bank digital currency project — involving China, Hong Kong, the UAE, and Thailand — reached minimum viable product in 2024. None of these alternatives can substitute for dollar clearing at scale. The point is that they are being built, and the pace of construction tracks directly with the frequency of US sanctions use.

Dollar reserve status rests on a specific expectation: that access to the network is politically neutral for routine transactions. Each sanctions episode is evidence against that expectation. The evidence accumulates. States that have not been sanctioned observe states that have been. The calculation is not complicated — what is the cost of reduced dollar exposure against the cost of remaining fully exposed? As coercive use of the network becomes more frequent and more indiscriminate, the first figure falls and the second rises.

Washington treats its financial coercion capacity as a stock it can draw on repeatedly. It is better understood as an endowment that depreciates with use. The 2022 freezing of Russian central bank reserves — roughly $300 billion held in Western custody — was the largest single depreciation event in the instrument's history. Every central bank with dollar holdings now holds a different asset than it held before February 2022: one demonstrated, concretely, to be conditional on political compliance. That demonstration cannot be undone.
