The Beijing Handshake: What the Trump–Xi Summit Did and Didn't Settle — Further Reading

May 2026 · Published 17 May 2026, 12:14 AM EST

The absence of a joint communiqué from Beijing tells you more than the presence of one ever would. When the United States and China genuinely agree on something, they say so in writing. When they don't, they issue statements about the quality of the conversation. What emerged from this summit was the latter — a performance of diplomatic normalcy that both sides needed for different reasons, which is not the same thing as diplomacy actually working.

To understand why, it helps to recall the structural tension that has governed every U.S.-China summit since at least 2017. American presidents arrive in Beijing carrying a domestic audience that wants toughness and a business community that wants access. Chinese leaders receive them carrying a domestic audience that wants sovereignty respected and a foreign policy establishment that treats any American concession as a precedent to be locked in. These incentives are not compatible, which is why summits tend to produce warmth and very little movement.

Taiwan is the fault line that makes all other issues secondary. The United States has maintained a policy of strategic ambiguity on Taiwan's defense for more than five decades — neither promising to defend the island nor promising to abandon it. That ambiguity has served as a stabilizing fiction. What analysts flagged from this summit was that Trump's failure to reaffirm pending arms sales to Taipei — sales that were already approved and in the pipeline — represented a quiet departure from that posture. It is the kind of concession that doesn't show up in any communiqué but registers clearly in Beijing, Taipei, and every Asian capital watching the relationship for signals about American reliability.

The confirmation that Xi Jinping will visit the United States in the fall introduces a second act to whatever this summit started. That visit will carry its own pressure to produce something tangible. The question is whether the fall timeline creates productive urgency or simply gives both sides another deadline to run down without resolution. Historical precedent is not encouraging. The 2023 Woodside summit between Xi and Biden generated substantial goodwill and resulted in the restoration of military-to-military communications — a meaningful but narrow outcome after years of near-total breakdown. A pattern of summits producing process rather than substance has become the relationship's equilibrium.

The Iran dimension is worth tracking separately. Beijing has significant economic relationships with Tehran and has generally resisted American pressure to use that leverage coercively. Trump's failure to extract any Chinese commitment on Iran — at a moment when Persian Gulf tensions are directly affecting global energy markets — suggests that China sees its Iran relationships as a strategic asset it has no interest in trading for better optics with Washington. That posture is unlikely to change regardless of how many state dinners are held.

What the summit did accomplish is the harder thing to dismiss. Personal relationships between heads of state matter at the margin, and Trump and Xi have now met multiple times across two presidencies. The fall visit, if it happens, will extend that contact. In a relationship where miscalculation is the primary risk, sustained communication has genuine value even when it produces nothing you can announce. The danger is in mistaking the communication for the agreement — in treating the handshake as the settlement when it is, at best, a placeholder.
