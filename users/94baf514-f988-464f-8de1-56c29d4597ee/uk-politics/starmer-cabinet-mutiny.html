Starmer Loses Four Junior Ministers as Leadership Threshold Looms

May 2026 · Published 13 May 2026, 12:15 AM EST

Four junior ministers resigned from Sir Keir Starmer's government on May 12 in a coordinated move that has pushed Labour to within striking distance of the formal threshold needed to trigger a leadership challenge. Miatta Fahnbulleh (devolution, faith and communities), Jess Phillips (safeguarding), Alex Davies-Jones (victims), and Zubir Ahmed (junior health) all cited a loss of confidence in the prime minister. Fahnbulleh's resignation letter said the government had not acted "with the vision, pace and ambition that our mandate for change demands." Ahmed, a Streeting ally, framed his exit around Scottish Labour and devolved electoral risk.

The trigger is the May 8 local elections, in which Labour lost approximately 1,500 council seats while Reform UK gained around 1,454 — figures based on media tallies on results night and subject to certification. LabourList's public tracker put at least 83 of Labour's 403 MPs on record calling for Starmer to stand down by Tuesday evening, with ITV News counting 92 prepared to back an ouster. A formal Labour leadership challenge requires 81 MPs — 20 percent of the parliamentary party — to nominate a challenger. The threshold is now within range of the public tally.

Starmer remains defiant. At Tuesday's cabinet he told colleagues the country "expects us to get on with governing" and described his government as a "10-year project of renewal." David Lammy is publicly backing him; ITV News reports more than 100 MPs have signed a letter saying "this is no time for a leadership contest." Wes Streeting, the most-named potential successor, is meeting Starmer on Wednesday. Junior-minister vacancies have been filled — Nesil Caliskan, Natalie Fleet, Catherine Atkinson and Preet Kaur Gill all confirmed Tuesday evening — preserving the appearance of a functioning government for the State Opening.

That State Opening is today. The King's Speech is scheduled for approximately 11:30am in the Lords and is expected to set out more than 35 bills, including measures on immigration, public services, and what the House of Commons Library describes as a refresh of the government's economic-growth agenda. Lords debates on the speech run May 14–21, with foreign affairs and defence scheduled for May 21.

Watch the count: if the public 83 becomes a private 81 nomination, the contest mechanism activates regardless of how the King's Speech lands. The next visible test is the May 14 Commons debate on the address.
