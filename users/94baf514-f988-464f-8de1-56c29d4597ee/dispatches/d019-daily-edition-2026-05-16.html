The Warsh Inheritance: A Fed Chair Arrives Into a Rate-Hike Storm

Kevin Warsh inherits a Federal Reserve that is no longer certain about anything. Jerome Powell's tenure ended May 15, and the man stepping into the chair arrives not at a moment of drift but at one of acute institutional pressure — Treasury yields at near-one-year highs of 4.55%, and market-implied odds of a rate hike sometime this year that have vaulted from under 1% a month ago to 45%, according to data tracked by Schwab and reported across Fortune and 24/7 Wall St. That is not a gradual recalibration. That is the bond market sending a signal it has been suppressing for months, now suddenly unable to suppress it. The proximate drivers are two: a war-related oil shock pushing energy inflation back into headline readings, and an Empire State Manufacturing print that came in stronger than expected, complicating the narrative that the economy is soft enough to justify cuts. Warsh's own positioning makes the situation more volatile still. He has reportedly made no promises to the White House on rate cuts — a pointed signal given that the political pressure from the Trump administration for lower rates has been sustained and public. That posture will be read two ways simultaneously: as independence, which markets formally reward, and as a potential collision course with a president who treats the Fed's rate decisions as a lever he is owed. What makes Warsh's inheritance structurally interesting is that the case for hiking and the case for cutting are both, in their own terms, coherent right now. If the Hormuz disruption sustains Brent above $100 and feeds through to CPI, a Fed that holds or cuts risks embedding an inflation expectation it cannot easily dislodge. But if the growth picture softens — and there are leading indicators pointing both directions — hiking into a slowing economy replicates the policy error the Fed has been accused of making in both directions across the last four years. The difference between those two paths is not just economic; it is institutional. A Fed that hikes under a president demanding cuts will face a legitimacy battle unlike anything Powell navigated, because Warsh will not have Powell's accumulated credibility as a buffer. His first press conference, his first vote, and his first public disagreement with the White House — whenever it comes — will each be read as a founding document of what this Fed actually is. The bond market is already writing the first draft.

No Deal, No Relief: The Beijing Summit and the Market It Couldn't Calm

The Trump-Xi Beijing summit ended without the deal the market had priced in, and equities made their disappointment immediate. The S&P 500 shed 1.24% and the Nasdaq fell 1.54% on Friday, according to CNBC and TheStreet, as traders who had held positions through two days of diplomatic theatre received a result that was neither rupture nor resolution — the particular outcome markets find hardest to price. Xi reportedly assured Trump that China would not supply military equipment to Iran, which is a meaningful concession in the context of the US-Iran conflict but not a structural trade agreement, not a Hormuz framework, and not the kind of deliverable that changes the inflation arithmetic currently running through every central bank in the developed world. The Boeing aircraft deal that did emerge from the summit illustrates the gap between diplomatic optics and market expectations precisely. Reports had circulated ahead of the summit that a 500-plane deal was in negotiation; the figure that materialised was closer to 200 planes. That is not nothing — it is a real commercial transaction and a face-saving gesture on both sides — but markets had positioned for the larger number, and the delta between 500 and 200 carries a meaning beyond the arithmetic. It signals that the summit was managed for optics on both sides rather than designed to produce durable economic re-engagement. CaixaBank Research noted in its post-summit analysis that the structural trade architecture between the two economies remains unchanged, with existing tariff schedules and export control regimes all still in place. Meanwhile, Brent crude sat at $105 per barrel through the weekend, driven less by the summit's outcome than by the Hormuz situation that the summit was partly convened to address. Tanker rerouting around the Cape of Good Hope is now at its highest levels since early 2025, a logistical adjustment that adds cost and transit time to every barrel that would otherwise move through the strait. That cost does not disappear because two leaders shook hands in Beijing. What the summit has produced, in structural terms, is a temporary floor beneath the conflict — a documented moment in which China has publicly distanced itself from Iranian military supply chains — but not a ceiling on the disruption itself. The next test of that assurance will not come in a diplomatic communiqué; it will come in tanker traffic data over the next three to four weeks.

Knife's Edge: Cleveland, San Antonio, and the Postseason at Its Best

The 2026 NBA Playoffs conference semifinals have arrived at the moment the format was designed to produce. Cleveland and Detroit went to overtime in Game 5, the Cavaliers closing a 9-0 run late in the fourth quarter to force the extra period before outlasting the top-seeded Pistons 117-113, according to CBS Sports and NBA.com. The Cavs now lead the series 3-2 heading back to Cleveland for Game 6, which means Detroit — the one seed, at home all postseason — faces elimination on the road. That reversal of structural advantage is the story of this series. The Pistons entered the playoffs as the conference's best record, a team that had constructed its identity around home dominance, and Cleveland has now won twice in Detroit. The mechanism of the comeback matters as much as the result. A 9-0 run in the final minutes of a playoff game is not a slow erosion; it is a collective decision to apply pressure at the exact moment when pressure is hardest to sustain. That kind of run requires multiple players to make consecutive correct decisions under conditions designed to prevent it. In the West, Victor Wembanyama posted 27 points and 17 rebounds as San Antonio blew out Minnesota 126-97 to take a 3-2 series lead, according to CBS Sports and NBA.com, setting up a potential Spurs-Thunder Western Conference Finals if San Antonio closes in Game 6. Wembanyama at this point in the postseason is doing something the league has not seen since its last generational center arrived: he is making the game look geometrically different when he is on the floor. The 17 rebounds against a Timberwolves team that is not small is a function of length and anticipation that no scheme fully neutralises. The Oklahoma City Thunder wait on the other side, having swept the Lakers to go 8-0 in the postseason, a record that is as much a statement about depth and system as it is about any single player. A Thunder-Spurs Western Conference Finals would pit the league's most efficient offensive system against the player best positioned to make that efficiency irrelevant on any given night. Game 6 of both series arrives Friday, and before the weekend ends, the bracket will either be set or pushed to a deciding game that neither team has earned the right to lose.

The $95 Billion Question: What Cerebras's Debut Tells Us About AI Valuation

A 190x revenue multiple is not a valuation — it is a thesis statement. Cerebras Systems closed its Nasdaq debut up 68% on May 14, reaching a market capitalisation of approximately $95 billion against 2025 revenue of just over $500 million, according to reporting from CNBC and Schwab. The number is striking not because AI-adjacent valuations have been conservative — they have not — but because of what it implies about the market's theory of the company. At 190x revenue, the price is not a discounted cashflow calculation with aggressive assumptions. It is a declaration that the revenue figure itself is almost beside the point, that what is being priced is positioning within an infrastructure buildout whose returns have not yet materialised but whose scale is already visible. The structural argument for that view is not irrational. Cerebras is building wafer-scale chips designed specifically for large language model training and inference — hardware optimised for the exact workload that every major technology company is currently scaling at extraordinary cost. If the AI infrastructure cycle runs another three to five years at its current pace, the companies that have secured dominant positions in the supply chain for that cycle could generate revenue multiples that make the current entry point look reasonable in retrospect. The counterargument is embedded in the word if. The IPO arrived, as CNBC noted, at a moment when AI-linked equities continued to lead US market gains even as broader tech pulled back under yield pressure on Friday — a divergence that reflects genuine uncertainty about whether AI infrastructure investment is being priced on evidence of returns or on the scale of the commitment alone. The distinction matters because those two pricing logics produce different outcomes when the cycle turns. Revenue multiples supported by demonstrated cashflow compress slowly; multiples supported by narrative compress fast. What the Cerebras debut has done is raise the question publicly in a way that secondary trading of existing names had not forced. An IPO sets a price that is then immediately tested by a market that did not set it, and 68% on day one is the market saying it endorses the thesis — for now. The real test arrives with the first earnings report, which will be the first moment the thesis has to survive contact with reported numbers.

The Chokepoint Economy: How the Strait of Hormuz Became the World's Central Bank

The Strait of Hormuz has always been a chokepoint, but it has become something more precise in 2026: the single variable around which global monetary policy is being forced to reorganise. The US-Iran conflict has entered what analysts quoted by Geopolitical Futures are calling a blockade diplomacy phase — a state of sustained, managed disruption in which neither side escalates to open conflict but neither permits the strait to function normally. Tanker traffic has rerouted around the Cape of Good Hope at its highest levels since early 2025, according to CaixaBank Research, adding approximately two weeks and several percentage points of cost to every cargo that would otherwise transit through the Gulf. A vessel was seized near the UAE coast reportedly heading toward Iranian waters, the kind of incident that does not resolve the underlying standoff but adds another data point to a sequence that tanker operators are treating as a pattern. Brent crude at $105 per barrel is the arithmetic consequence. That number flows forward through the economy in ways that are not symmetric and not easily reversed. Energy costs raise headline CPI immediately; they feed into core through transport, manufacturing inputs, and logistics with a lag; and they reduce real consumer purchasing power in ways that look, from the output side, like demand weakness. This is the stagflation combination that central banks are structurally least equipped to handle, because the instrument they use to fight inflation — raising rates — also compounds the demand weakness that the same inflation is already producing. The World Economic Forum has flagged the Hormuz situation as the primary external shock now forcing central banks from Washington to Frankfurt into that territory. For the Fed, this lands on Kevin Warsh's desk in his first week. For the ECB, it arrives against a European growth picture that was already fragile before energy costs reaccelerated. The geopolitical mechanism here is what makes it analytically different from a standard supply shock. A hurricane or an earthquake closes a port; the closure has a duration that markets can model. Blockade diplomacy does not have a duration — it has conditions, and the conditions are political. The strait does not reopen when a storm passes; it reopens when a negotiated framework permits it to, which means the oil price is not a function of geology or weather but of decisions being made in Tehran, Washington, and Beijing simultaneously. How long that three-way calculation takes to resolve will determine whether $105 Brent is the ceiling of this shock or the floor.
