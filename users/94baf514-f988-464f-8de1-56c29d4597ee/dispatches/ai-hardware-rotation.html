Wall Street's AI Chip Rotation

May 2026 · Published 12 May 2026, 12:12 AM EST

Wall Street's AI-hardware trade has rotated. CNBC reported on Friday that AMD, Intel, and Micron have each surged into double-digit weekly gains while Nvidia — the four-year dominant beneficiary of AI compute capital — has lagged the broader Nasdaq, prompting analysts to describe a changing of the guard in AI. For the year to date, AMD is up approximately 112%, Intel up roughly 240%, and Micron has reached new highs, while Nvidia is up about 15%, barely ahead of the index.

The repositioning has a specific mechanism. The earlier phase of the AI build-out concentrated capital in GPU training infrastructure, where Nvidia is structurally dominant. The current phase — driven by the production deployment of agentic AI and inference workloads — runs through CPUs and high-bandwidth memory. Bank of America projects the data-center CPU market more than doubling from $27 billion in 2025 to $60 billion in 2030, and Micron's CEO told CNBC in March that major customers are receiving only half to two-thirds of the high-bandwidth memory they have ordered. AMD's Q1 2026 results on May 5 gave the rotation an earnings anchor: revenue of $10.3 billion (+38% year-on-year), data-centre revenue of $5.8 billion (+57%), and a revised forecast lifting expected server-CPU market growth from 18% to 35% annually through 2030 — implying a $120 billion server-CPU revenue pool by decade's end. AMD CEO Lisa Su described the quarter as "a structural shift in our business."

Nvidia's response, disclosed in the same window, is a pivot to becoming the AI sector's largest equity backer rather than just its hardware supplier. The company has now committed more than $40 billion in AI equity investments in the first four months of 2026, anchored by a $30 billion stake in OpenAI announced in late February, followed by deals for up to $3.2 billion in Corning and up to $2.1 billion in IREN, alongside an earlier $2 billion CoreWeave position now valued at roughly $4.4 billion. The strategy moves Nvidia upstream of its customers: financing the data-centre buildout and memory supply chain that must consume its GPUs.

Nvidia reports fiscal Q1 2027 earnings on May 28 — the next test of whether the rotation reflects a durable shift in where AI margins accrue or a temporary repricing of laggards against an outsized incumbent.
