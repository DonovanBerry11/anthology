Iran War Fuel Crisis: Europe Has Weeks of Jet Fuel Left as Brent Hits 14

May 2026 · Published 18 May 2026, 1:00 PM EST

The closure of the Strait of Hormuz — effectively sealed since late February 2026, when the US-Israel air campaign against Iran began — has produced an energy crisis that IEA Executive Director Fatih Birol has called "the largest energy crisis we have ever faced." Brent crude surged past $120 per barrel following the strait's closure before settling near $114 in early May, a rise of approximately 55% since the start of the conflict.

The most acute near-term consequence is a jet fuel shortage in Europe. The Middle East previously supplied approximately 75% of Europe's net jet fuel imports. In mid-April, Birol warned that Europe had "maybe six weeks" of jet fuel reserves remaining if the strait did not reopen — a statement the EU Commission initially moved to downplay. By late April, global jet fuel exports had plunged approximately 30%, to 1.3 million barrels per day, from 1.9 million bpd in the same period last year. European refineries are operating at maximum capacity and cannot absorb the shortfall domestically. The International Air Transport Association estimated that flight cancellations in Europe could begin by end of May.

The timeline now sits in an uncertain holding pattern. On May 4, the Trump administration launched "Project Freedom" — a US Navy-escorted maritime corridor designed to guide commercial vessels through the strait. It lasted less than 48 hours. On May 6, Trump paused the operation, citing "great progress" toward a "complete and final agreement" with Iran and saying the blockade would remain in force while negotiations continued. As of May 17, the strait remains closed to commercial traffic.

The pause puts Europe's summer travel season in direct exposure to the negotiating timeline. Airlines heading into their heaviest operating schedules of the year — June through August — are now pricing routes under the assumption that fuel costs will remain elevated and that some degree of capacity reduction is unavoidable. ConocoPhillips, the third-largest US oil producer, has warned that import-dependent countries could face critical shortages by June or July if the strait does not reopen.

Watch the Iran deal talks: if an agreement is not formalised within the next two to three weeks, the IEA's six-week warning will have expired, and Europe will be in the critical reserve range coinciding with peak summer demand.
