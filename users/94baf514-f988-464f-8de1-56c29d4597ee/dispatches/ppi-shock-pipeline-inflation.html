PPI Hits 6% as Pipeline Inflation Builds Into Warsh's First Meeting

May 2026 · Published 14 May 2026, 12:11 AM EST

Wholesale prices accelerated sharply in April, with the Producer Price Index rising to 6.0% year-on-year from an upwardly revised 4.3% in March — the hottest reading since December 2022 and roughly two percentage points above the consensus estimate. The release on Wednesday lands twenty-four hours after April CPI printed at 3.8%, also the highest since May 2023, and frames the inflation problem as one of pipeline pressure rather than a single hot data point. Producer prices typically lead consumer prices by one to three months.

The composition of the PPI release is what is consequential. According to Benzinga's analysis of the BLS detail, unprocessed energy materials rose 9.2% in the month and are now up 48.9% on the year, with crude petroleum at 11.3%. But the pressure extends beyond the energy line: core PPI rose 1.0% month-on-month, lifting the annual core rate to 5.2%, and roughly 60% of the headline gain came from a 1.2% advance in final-demand services rather than from goods. Services inflation is the variable the Fed has consistently flagged as the most persistent.

The combined release reshapes the rates outlook directly. CME FedWatch futures no longer price in any rate cuts in 2026, a reversal from the start of the year when at least one quarter-point cut was the consensus. The 10-year Treasury yield rose modestly to 4.48% after a one percent climb the previous session. Kevin Warsh, confirmed Wednesday as the next Fed chair by a 54-45 Senate vote, inherits an FOMC that just dissented four ways over including an easing bias in its statement, and a data set in which both the proximate (CPI) and leading (PPI) inflation indicators are accelerating into his first meeting on June 16–17.

Two specific channels of pass-through matter for the May print. The first is energy, with WTI above $100 and gasoline at $4.50 a gallon nationally per AAA, both tied to the Iran conflict and the Hormuz transit disruption. The second is tariff-related goods costs that have not yet fully cleared the producer level. Economists raised their May CPI expectations on the back of Wednesday's PPI release.

The BLS publishes May CPI on June 11 and May PPI on June 12, five and four days respectively before the FOMC decision.
