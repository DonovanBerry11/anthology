A Summit Without a Settlement: What the Beijing Talks Did — and Didn't — Resolve — Further Reading

May 2026 · Published 16 May 2026, 12:13 AM EST

The architecture of a US-China summit is almost always more revealing than its output. Both governments invest enormous energy in managing what a meeting appears to resolve, because the appearance of stability is itself a deliverable — one that moves markets, steadies allies, and buys time for domestic audiences. What happened in Beijing fits a pattern that stretches back to the Nixon era: when the two powers cannot agree on substance, they agree on tone, and they let the joint communiqué do the diplomatic work that actual concessions cannot. The 1972 Shanghai Communiqué, often held up as a masterstroke, was itself a document built around an artful disagreement — the United States 'acknowledging' rather than 'recognising' Beijing's position on Taiwan, a distinction so carefully worded that both sides could claim it meant what they needed it to mean. The summit just concluded operates in that same tradition. The Taiwan question has never been more structurally entangled with US domestic politics than it is now. Any president who explicitly shelves an arms package risks a ferocious response from Congress, where Taiwan support is one of the few genuinely bipartisan positions remaining. But any president who proceeds with the package immediately after a summit where Xi made his concerns explicit risks collapsing whatever goodwill the meeting generated. Trump's statement that he 'has not yet decided' is therefore not ambiguity — it is the policy. The Iran dimension adds a layer that previous US-China summits did not have to manage. China is Iran's largest oil customer and has an obvious economic interest in keeping Iranian crude flowing. The United States, conducting what amounts to a naval and air campaign that has already driven global oil above a hundred dollars, needs Chinese restraint in resupplying or financially backstopping Tehran. Neither side can say this plainly in public without domestic political cost, which is why the joint statements refer instead to 'regional stability' and 'de-escalation pathways.' It is worth understanding that the Strait of Hormuz closure, if it becomes permanent, would not affect China and the United States equally. Roughly a fifth of global oil shipment transits the strait. China, which imports heavily from the Gulf, would face severe supply pressure. The United States, now a net energy exporter, would face price pain but not supply constraint. This asymmetry gives Washington more leverage than Beijing would like to acknowledge, and it almost certainly structured what Xi was willing to say behind closed doors versus what he was willing to commit to on paper. Summit diplomacy between these two powers has functioned for decades as a pressure-release valve rather than a settlement mechanism. The real agreements tend to happen in quieter channels — military hotlines, treasury back-channels, and the conversations between trade ministers that never appear in any readout. What a summit does, at its most functional, is keep those quieter channels open by demonstrating that the relationship has not broken down entirely. By that minimal standard, Beijing 2026 succeeded. By any larger standard, it deferred.
