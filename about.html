Media is a system of information publishing, information filtration and compression, and information consumption. By filtration and compression, I mean filtering and compressing the stock of published media into something digestible and consumable given humans' limited attentional bandwidth.

In pre-internet media, this media flow was a one-way process. Large, centralised publishing institutions — print media publications, large radio and television networks — enjoyed a monopoly on publishing, largely owing to prohibitively expensive fixed capital costs involved in prevailing media technologies. In order to publish media, one had to be employed by one of these centralised publishers as a writer, journalist, or television anchor. The rest of the masses could only consume media, and had no access to the publishing infrastructure. Control of publishing and filtration were one and the same: centralised publishers filtered media by nature of dictating which individuals could publish in the first place, as well as their editorial power over what individuals attempted to publish. There was a scarce amount of page space and a scarce amount of air time, and one had to go through centralised publishers to obtain a share of that scarce infrastructure.

With the rise of the digital, this all changed. Anyone with an internet connection could, in theory, publish to the web — create a webpage, publish a blog, or ultimately create a social media account and publish there. Of course, there was no guarantee that this publication was met with attention. In pre-internet media, there was a scarcity of publishing infrastructure which severely constrained access to it — however, this also meant that, once access was obtained, there was limited competition for the attention of media consumers. If the New York Times publishes my op-ed, I am likely to receive far more attention than if I buy a domain name, publish my op-ed there, and wait for readers.

In short, filtration became detached from publishing. In the pre-internet era, filtration occurred naturally as a result of publishing infrastructure scarcity — but with the decentralisation and abundance of publishing infrastructure, filtration became a chokepoint in itself. This chokepoint was solved to various extents by search engines, social media social graphs, and ultimately social media engagement-based algorithms — interest graphs.1 In order for a decentralised publisher to source attention, they needed to publish content targeted for SEO, the construction of a social following, or engagement-based algorithms. Publishing infrastructure was now abundant, but attention was not — thus, the act of publishing was no longer constrained by centralised publishers, but by the filtration mechanisms dictated by the likes of Alphabet, Meta, and xAI. To the extent that publishers seek the attention of media consumers, this did not represent a democratisation in publishing, but rather a regime shift in the overarching constraint structure governing their ability to obtain attention for their published media.

Similarly, from the perspective of a media consumer, this did not represent a democratisation in information access. Consumers increasingly found themselves in an environment of new media consumption interfaces — the algorithmic newsfeed, infinite scroll — and new institutions influencing the media likely to make its way into their attentional space. Any published media which gained their attention had to have passed through the filtration mechanisms designed and implemented by large, profit-seeking platform operators.

So, what now? LLM technology presents a novel tool not just for filtration — that is, as a language-based interface for surfacing an individual piece of media — but also for compression. By compression, I mean distilling the linguistic patterns inherent in a near-boundless corpus of published text into a digestible piece of media that suits individuals' limited attentional bandwidth.

However, this compression function still largely requires active prompting by the media consumer. LLM compression is more analogous to search than it is to algorithmic feed delivery, or pre-internet media formats like newspapers and television. There exists no widespread interface for low-effort delivery of LLM-compressed media.

This is where Anthology comes in. We are creating a system for low-effort consumption of LLM-compressed media, tailored to the taste of each specific user. This is the next step in media evolution: bespoke delivery of compressed media for low-effort consumption. Whereas an algorithmic feed takes an indigestibly large corpus of media and filters out digestible, individual pieces that fit the preferences of individual users, we compress that corpus into synthesised pieces of media that fit the preferences of individual users.

And, as opposed to the user interface of a scrollable feed — which suits navigation through algorithmically filtered pieces of individual media — LLM-based compression and synthesis lends itself to traditional media interfaces like the newspaper, which revolve less around individuation and granularisation. We expect LLM-based advancements in underlying media technology can work towards a reversal into traditional forms of media:2 in contrast to the anxiety of an infinitely updating algorithmic feed, we want users to experience the ease of leaning back with a newspaper, with that newspaper specifically tailored to their individual interests.

This is not to de-emphasise individual pieces of media directly attributable to individual publishers. Human-generated media will always be a core facet of the media ecosystem. That will never change. However, as the quantity of available digital media in the world continues to expand exponentially — increasingly outstripping our limited psychological bandwidth to consume and digest it — we will increasingly require means of parcelling out digestible subsets of that media for individual consumers. We are simply extending LLM capabilities into low-effort consumption: replacing the fragmented noise of the feed with the structured clarity of the paper. We see LLM-generated media and human-generated media as co-existing in a symbiotic relationship, complementing and strengthening each other. And we believe in transparency in clearly labelling LLM- versus human-generated media.

As this symbiotic relationship between LLM-generated and human-generated media proceeds, we expect it to facilitate the growth of what evolutionary biologist Joseph Henrich has termed our collective brains3 — the collective knowledge of human society, progressively accumulated over generations via cultural transmission across space and time. LLM-based compression and synthesis will be key in helping us navigate an increasingly complex information environment, and will be a necessary technological underpinning of our generational accumulation of knowledge. It is this generational knowledge accumulation that has led Brad DeLong to refer to our species as "the Anthology Super-Intelligence that is all of us humans thinking together."4

We look forward to being a part of this journey with Anthology.
